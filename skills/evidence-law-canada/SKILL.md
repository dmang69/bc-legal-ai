# Evidence Law — Canadian and BC Practice

The gatekeeping rules for what a court or tribunal can consider. Getting
evidence in — and keeping opposing evidence out — is often where cases
are decided.

## Module 1: Relevance and Materiality

Evidence is admissible only if it is:

**Material** — the fact it tends to prove is one that matters to the
issues in the proceeding, as defined by the pleadings and the applicable
law.

**Relevant** — the evidence makes a material fact more or less probable
than it would be without the evidence.

Both are thresholds. Evidence that fails either is inadmissible.

### The Relevance Test in Practice

Ask, of each piece of evidence:

1. What fact does this evidence tend to prove?
2. Is that fact one of the issues in the case (materiality)?
3. Does the evidence actually tend to prove it (relevance)?

### The Probative-Prejudicial Balance

Even relevant, material evidence may be excluded where its probative
value is substantially outweighed by prejudicial effect. In criminal
cases this doctrine (Corbett, Handy, Seaboyer) is well developed. In
civil cases it is applied more sparingly but exists.

## Module 2: Admissibility vs Weight

**Admissibility** is a legal question: does the evidence pass the rules
so it may be received?

**Weight** is a factual question: how persuasive is the evidence once
received?

Confusing these is a common error. Admissible evidence may still be
worthless. Excluded evidence may have been decisive.

The distinction matters procedurally:

- Admissibility objections must be raised at the time
- Weight is argued at the end
- On appeal, admissibility errors are questions of law
- Weight determinations receive deference

## Module 3: Hearsay — The General Rule and the Principled Approach

### The Definition

A hearsay statement is an out-of-court statement offered to prove the
truth of its contents. The declarant is not available for
cross-examination on the statement.

### The General Rule

Hearsay is presumptively inadmissible.

Reason: the trier of fact cannot assess the declarant's perception,
memory, sincerity, and narration through cross-examination.

### The Principled Approach (post-Khan, Khelawon)

Hearsay may be admitted where the party seeking admission establishes:

1. **Necessity** — the evidence cannot otherwise be obtained in a form
   that permits cross-examination (declarant dead, unavailable, refuses,
   or cross-examination would be futile)

2. **Reliability** — either:
   - Threshold procedural reliability (circumstances of the statement
     provide adequate substitutes for cross-examination), OR
   - Threshold substantive reliability (the statement is so trustworthy
     that cross-examination would add little)

The principled approach can admit hearsay that no traditional exception
covers, and can EXCLUDE hearsay that a traditional exception would
admit if the principled analysis fails.

### The Modern Framework

The test is on the party proposing to adduce the hearsay. Voir dire may
be needed. The court makes a threshold reliability finding; the
ultimate weight remains for the trier of fact.

Key cases: *Khan* 1990 SCC (child sexual assault, present sense
impression), *Smith* 1992 SCC, *B(KG)* 1993 SCC (prior inconsistent
statements), *Starr* 2000 SCC, *Khelawon* 2006 SCC (unified framework),
*Baldree* 2013 SCC.

## Module 4: Traditional Hearsay Exceptions Still in Use

Even under the principled approach, traditional exceptions provide
useful shortcuts. Most survive as presumptively-admissible categories
subject to the principled overlay.

### Business Records

Records made in the ordinary course of business, contemporaneously,
by someone with knowledge or duty to record.

- Common law: *Ares v Venner* 1970 SCC
- Statute: Canada Evidence Act s.30, BC Evidence Act s.42

### Public Records

Official records made by public officials in the discharge of a duty.

### Statements of Present Intention

A declarant's statement of their own present intention, offered as
evidence they carried out that intention.

### Statements Against Interest

A statement against the pecuniary, proprietary, or (in some
jurisdictions) penal interest of the declarant when made.

### Dying Declarations

Statements made under settled hopeless expectation of death, concerning
the cause or circumstances of death. Very narrow application.

### Statements in Prior Judicial Proceedings

Testimony given in a prior proceeding may be admissible where the party
against whom offered had opportunity to cross-examine. Specific rules
apply.

### Res Gestae — Spontaneous Statements

Statements so closely connected to a startling event as to negate
opportunity for reflection or fabrication. Absorbed largely into the
principled approach.

### Statements as to Bodily and Mental State

Contemporaneous statements about the declarant's own physical
condition, feelings, or mental state.

### Ancient Documents

Documents 20+ years old, found in proper custody, with no marks of
tampering. Rarely invoked in modern practice.

## Module 5: Privilege

Privilege protects certain communications from disclosure. Privilege
belongs to the holder (usually the client, not the lawyer), and only
the holder can waive it.

### Solicitor-Client Privilege

Communications between a lawyer (acting as such) and a client, for the
purpose of legal advice, made in confidence.

- Nearly absolute; can only be pierced in exceptional circumstances
  (innocence at stake, imminent threat to public safety, statutory
  authority)
- Survives the death of the client (generally)
- Waived by disclosure to third parties (but joint privilege
  arrangements possible)

*Descôteaux* 1982 SCC, *McClure* 2001 SCC, *Blood Tribe* 2008 SCC.

### Litigation Privilege

Documents created for the dominant purpose of pending or contemplated
litigation.

- Extends to communications with third parties (unlike solicitor-client)
- Ends when the litigation ends (Blank v Canada 2006 SCC)
- Applies to work product prepared for the litigation

### Settlement Privilege ("Without Prejudice")

Communications made in genuine attempt to settle a dispute are protected
from disclosure. Purpose: to encourage settlement.

- Applies whether or not the words "without prejudice" appear, if the
  purpose is clear
- Applies to communications between counsel or parties
- Exceptions: proving settlement was reached, misconduct, unconscionability

*Sable Offshore Energy* 2013 SCC.

### Spousal Privilege

At common law, a spouse was privileged not to disclose communications
made during marriage. Now largely codified and modified. The Canada
Evidence Act and provincial evidence acts have specific provisions.

Recent developments have limited spousal incompetence and privilege in
various contexts.

### Case-by-Case ("Wigmore") Privilege

Communications not fitting an established category may be privileged
where:

1. Communication originated in confidence
2. Confidentiality is essential to the relationship
3. The relationship is one society should sedulously foster
4. Injury to the relationship from disclosure would outweigh the
   benefit of correct disposal

*Slavutych v Baker* 1976 SCC; applied to journalist sources, sexual
assault counselling records, and other categories.

### Public Interest Immunity

The Crown may claim protection for documents where disclosure would harm
the public interest. Court weighs disclosure against harm. Governed by
Canada Evidence Act ss.37-39 for federal matters.

### Informer Privilege

Nearly absolute protection of the identity of police informers, subject
only to the "innocence at stake" exception.

## Module 6: Best Evidence Rule and Authentication

### Best Evidence Rule

Historical rule requiring production of the original document rather
than a copy. Substantially relaxed in modern practice — copies are
routinely admissible, subject to authentication.

### Authentication

A document must be authenticated as being what it is claimed to be.
Methods:

- Testimony of a witness with personal knowledge
- Signature identification
- Distinctive characteristics
- Circumstantial evidence
- Testimony that document was found in a place where it would be
  expected

### Electronic Documents

Governed by evidence act provisions on electronic records. The party
seeking admission must establish the reliability of the recording
system.

- BC Evidence Act s.41.1–41.7 (electronic records)
- Canada Evidence Act s.31.1–31.8

## Module 7: Judicial Notice

The court's acceptance of a fact without formal proof.

### Categories

**Notorious facts** — facts so well known that they cannot reasonably be
disputed. E.g. that Vancouver is in BC.

**Facts capable of immediate and accurate demonstration** — facts
verifiable by resort to sources of indisputable accuracy. E.g. the date
of a particular day of the week.

### Legislative and Adjudicative Facts

Distinction developed in *R v Spence* 2005 SCC.

- **Adjudicative facts** — facts about the specific parties and events.
  Higher standard for judicial notice.
- **Legislative or social facts** — facts about general context, social
  conditions, etc. Lower standard, more latitude to take notice.

### Foreign Law

Historically treated as fact requiring proof. Modern practice permits
judicial notice of some foreign law, particularly from other common-law
jurisdictions, subject to expert evidence for contested points.

## Module 8: Opinion Evidence — Lay and Expert

### Lay Opinion

Ordinary witnesses generally testify to facts, not opinions. Limited
exceptions for lay opinions on:

- Physical characteristics (age, weight, distance)
- Emotional state
- Speed of vehicles
- Identity based on familiar recognition
- Common conclusions any reasonable person would draw from the observed
  facts

### Expert Opinion — The Mohan Test

For expert opinion to be admissible:

1. **Relevance** — logically probative of a fact in issue
2. **Necessity** — outside the ordinary experience and knowledge of the
   trier of fact
3. **Absence of an exclusionary rule**
4. **Properly qualified expert** — special or peculiar knowledge

*R v Mohan* 1994 SCC.

### The Gatekeeping Function — White Burgess

Even where the Mohan criteria are met, the trial judge exercises a
residual gatekeeping discretion:

- Cost-benefit analysis: probative value vs prejudicial effect
- Reliability of the underlying methodology
- Impartiality and independence of the expert (*White Burgess Langille
  Inman v Abbott* 2015 SCC)

An expert who is not impartial and independent may be disqualified.

### Expert's Duty to the Court

- Duty of independence
- Duty to disclose bases for opinion
- Duty to consider alternative explanations
- Not to be an advocate for the retaining party

In BC Supreme Court, experts must file a certificate of independence
under Rule 11 (verify current text).

## Module 9: Character Evidence and Similar Fact

### Character Evidence

**Civil cases**: character evidence about a party is generally
irrelevant and inadmissible, subject to narrow exceptions where
character is directly in issue (e.g. defamation actions).

**Criminal cases**: accused may adduce evidence of good character;
Crown then may rebut. Complex rules govern character evidence about
non-accused persons.

### Similar Fact Evidence

Evidence of other misconduct by a party, offered for a purpose beyond
mere propensity. Presumptively inadmissible in criminal cases; assessed
under the *Handy* test.

Modern approach: probative value must substantially outweigh
prejudicial effect. Considerations:

- Similarity of the past conduct to the present allegation
- Frequency
- Circumstances
- Temporal proximity
- Distinctive features
- Availability of the evidence

*R v Handy* 2002 SCC.

In civil cases, similar-fact evidence is more freely admitted where
relevant, but the probative-prejudicial balance still applies (*Mood
Music Publishing v De Wolfe* [1976]; recognized in Canadian cases).

## Module 10: Prior Consistent and Inconsistent Statements

### Prior Consistent Statements

Generally inadmissible for the truth of their contents — cannot be used
to bolster a witness by showing they said the same thing before.

Exceptions:
- Rebutting an allegation of recent fabrication
- Prior identification
- Res gestae (spontaneous statements)
- Statements in narrative context to make evidence intelligible

### Prior Inconsistent Statements

Traditionally admissible only to impeach credibility, not for the truth.
Modified by *R v B(KG)* 1993 SCC — prior inconsistent statements may be
admitted for the truth if they meet the principled hearsay test.

For witnesses, statutes govern how prior inconsistent statements are
proven:

- Canada Evidence Act ss.10-11
- BC Evidence Act (verify current section)

## Module 11: Documentary Evidence

### Original vs Copy

Modern practice accepts copies routinely, subject to challenges as to
authenticity or specific rules requiring originals.

### Business Records

Statutory admission mechanisms:
- Canada Evidence Act s.30
- BC Evidence Act s.42

Requirements typically:
- Made in the usual and ordinary course of a business
- Contemporaneous or near-contemporaneous
- Made by someone with a duty to record

### Electronic Records

Governed by specific statutory provisions requiring proof of the
recording system's reliability.

### Chain of Custody

For documents whose authenticity or integrity is contested, chain of
custody may need to be established through a series of witnesses.

### Exhibits

Documents entered as evidence become exhibits. In affidavit-based
proceedings, exhibits are attached to affidavits and identified by the
affiant.

## Module 12: Evidence in Administrative Proceedings (Including RTB)

Administrative tribunals typically have more flexible evidentiary rules
than courts. But "flexibility" is not "no rules."

### Statutory Framework

Most administrative tribunals in BC operate under the Administrative
Tribunals Act (ATA), which permits admission of relevant evidence not
otherwise admissible in court.

### RTB Practice

Under the Residential Tenancy Act and the RTB Rules of Procedure:

- Evidence must be relevant to the issues
- Hearsay is admissible but its weight depends on reliability
- Documents are typically admitted through the party providing them
- Cross-examination is permitted in participatory hearings

### The Relaxed Rules — Trap for the Unwary

Because tribunals accept hearsay and other evidence courts would
exclude, parties sometimes assume "anything goes." Not so:

- Weight is still assessed
- Unreliable evidence receives little weight even if admitted
- Procedural fairness still requires notice and opportunity to respond
  to material evidence

### Building an Evidence Bundle for RTB

- Organize chronologically or by issue
- Number every document
- Provide an index
- Ensure copies for the arbitrator and other party
- Bring originals if authenticity may be challenged
