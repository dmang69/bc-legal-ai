## Common Objection Vocabulary

### Hearsay
"Objection, hearsay."
"Objection, hearsay — no exception applies and the principled test is
not met because [X]."

### Relevance
"Objection, relevance."
"Objection, the question calls for evidence not relevant to any issue
in this proceeding."

### Beyond Scope
"Objection, this exceeds the scope of direct."
"Objection, this exceeds the pleading."

### Argumentative
"Objection, argumentative."
"Objection, counsel is arguing with the witness."

### Compound Question
"Objection, compound question."

### Leading (on direct)
"Objection, leading question on direct examination."

### Speculation
"Objection, calls for speculation."

### Improper Character Evidence
"Objection, this is character evidence not going to any recognized
purpose."

### Improper Opinion
"Objection, lay opinion outside the recognized categories."

### Improper Expert Opinion
"Objection, this witness is not qualified to give this opinion."
"Objection, this opinion exceeds the scope of the expert's
qualifications / disclosed opinions."

### Privilege
"Objection, privileged. This calls for solicitor-client privileged
communication."

### Ambiguity
"Objection, ambiguous — I cannot determine what the witness is being
asked."

### Asked and Answered
"Objection, asked and answered."

### Assumes Facts Not in Evidence
"Objection, the question assumes a fact not established in evidence."

## Objection Discipline

- Object at the time — waiting waives
- State the specific ground
- Do not argue the objection unless invited
- Once ruled on, comply with the ruling and preserve the record
- If necessary, request that the record reflect the ruling

## After the Hearing

For each ruling against you:

- Note it in this log
- Consider whether it's grounds for appeal or JR
- Rulings on questions of law are more likely to be appealable than
  rulings on weight
