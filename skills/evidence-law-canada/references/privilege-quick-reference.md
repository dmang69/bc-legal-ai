# Privilege — Quick Reference

Expanded reference for the privilege doctrines that come up most in
Canadian civil practice.

---

## Solicitor-Client Privilege

**What it protects:** Communications between a lawyer and a client, for
the purpose of legal advice, made in confidence.

**Duration:** Permanent. Survives death of the client (in most cases).

**Who holds:** The client.

**Waiver:** Only the client can waive. Disclosure to third parties
generally waives, but joint retainer, common interest, and inadvertent
disclosure principles apply.

**Exceptions:**
- Innocence at stake (very narrow — see *McClure*)
- Imminent threat to public safety
- Statutory abrogation (rare and construed strictly)
- Crime-fraud exception (communications in furtherance of crime or fraud
  are not privileged)

**In practice:**
- Emails between lawyer and client → privileged
- Notes of legal advice → privileged
- The fact that legal advice was sought → generally privileged in Canada
  (unlike some US jurisdictions)
- The identity of the client → sometimes privileged, sometimes not

**Key cases:** *Descôteaux* 1982 SCC; *Solosky* 1980 SCC; *McClure*
2001 SCC; *Blood Tribe* 2008 SCC; *Chambre des notaires* 2016 SCC.

---

## Litigation Privilege

**What it protects:** Documents and communications created for the
dominant purpose of pending or contemplated litigation.

**Duration:** Ends when the litigation ends (*Blank v Canada* 2006 SCC).

**Who holds:** The party. Both party and lawyer can claim.

**Waiver:** Voluntary disclosure. Related-litigation exception can extend
protection in some circumstances.

**Distinguished from solicitor-client:**
- Solicitor-client: needs lawyer, no litigation required
- Litigation: no lawyer required, needs litigation as dominant purpose

**In practice:**
- Witness statements taken for litigation → privileged
- Expert reports prepared for litigation → generally privileged until
  expert is called
- Investigator reports for litigation → privileged
- Internal memos about strategy → privileged

**Key cases:** *Blank v Canada (Minister of Justice)* 2006 SCC;
*Lizotte v Aviva* 2016 SCC.

---

## Settlement Privilege ("Without Prejudice")

**What it protects:** Communications made in a genuine attempt to settle
a dispute.

**Duration:** Depends. In some cases permanent; in others may terminate
with the specific negotiation.

**Who holds:** Joint privilege of the negotiating parties. Both must
waive.

**Purpose:** To encourage frank settlement discussions without fear of
disclosure.

**Exceptions:**
- Proving that a settlement was reached
- Misconduct in the negotiations
- Unconscionability
- Statutory disclosure obligations

**In practice:**
- Formal offers marked "without prejudice" → privileged
- Informal settlement discussions → privileged if purpose is clear
- Communications after settlement → not privileged unless directly
  concerning the settlement

**Formula 1 rule / Calderbank offers:** Offers made "without prejudice
except as to costs" — protected from disclosure on the merits but
available on costs after judgment.

**Key case:** *Sable Offshore Energy* 2013 SCC.

## Spousal Privilege

**Current status:** Substantially modified by statute. The historical
common-law privilege has been narrowed.

**Federal:** Canada Evidence Act ss.3-4 and related provisions.

**BC:** BC Evidence Act contains relevant provisions.

**In practice:**
- Recent legislative changes have limited spousal incompetence in
  criminal proceedings
- Consult current statute text — this area has changed
- Same-sex spouses receive same treatment

**Note:** This is an area where the law has moved significantly. Verify
current provisions before relying on any specific rule.

## Case-by-Case ("Wigmore") Privilege

For confidential relationships not covered by class privileges. Four
criteria (from *Slavutych v Baker* 1976 SCC):

1. Communication originated in confidence
2. Confidentiality is essential to the relationship
3. The relationship is one society should sedulously foster
4. Injury to the relationship from disclosure would outweigh the benefit
   of correct disposal

**Applied to:**
- Journalist-source (*R v National Post* 2010 SCC — narrow test)
- Sexual assault counsellor records (statutory scheme in criminal)
- Some priest-penitent situations
- Peer-review discussions in some contexts

**Not applied to:**
- Doctor-patient (as a class — but specific communications may qualify)
- Accountant-client (generally not privileged in Canada)

## Informer Privilege

**Nearly absolute.** Protects the identity of persons who provide
information to police in confidence.

**One exception:** Innocence at stake in a criminal proceeding.

**Not waivable** by the informer alone — protects a public interest.

**Key case:** *Named Person v Vancouver Sun* 2007 SCC.

---

## Public Interest Immunity

The Crown may claim protection where disclosure would harm the public
interest.

**Federal:** Canada Evidence Act ss.37-39 (national security,
intergovernmental relations, cabinet confidence).

**Provincial:** Common-law and statutory provisions.

**Procedure:** Court weighs disclosure against harm, sometimes examining
the documents in camera.

---

## Aboriginal Communications

Communications with Elders or in Indigenous cultural contexts may
attract privilege under Wigmore or specific evolving doctrines.
Increasingly recognized area, particularly in matters affecting
Indigenous rights.

## Litigation Practice Tips

### Claiming Privilege

- Assert clearly and specifically
- If withholding documents, provide a privilege log describing the
  documents in enough detail for opposing counsel to assess the claim
- Do not include the content — that would waive privilege
- Log entry typically includes: date, author, recipient, general
  subject, privilege claimed

### Challenging Privilege Claims

- Request particulars if the privilege log is inadequate
- If the claim is questionable, apply for the court to review the
  documents in camera
- Consider whether waiver has occurred
- Consider whether the underlying communication was ever privileged

### Waiver Traps

Waiver can occur through:
- Voluntary disclosure to a non-privileged third party
- Using the privileged communication as a sword (e.g. relying on legal
  advice as a defence)
- Selective disclosure — cannot cherry-pick favorable parts
- Inadvertent disclosure (though relief may be available if prompt
  action taken)

### Common Interest Privilege

Parties with a common interest (e.g. co-defendants) may share
privileged communications without waiving privilege as against
third parties. Doctrine is more restricted in Canada than in some US
jurisdictions.

---

## Privilege in Administrative Proceedings

Privilege claims apply in administrative proceedings, though the
mechanisms may differ from court. At the RTB and similar tribunals:

- Solicitor-client privilege applies with full force
- Settlement privilege applies
- Public interest immunity may be raised by government participants
- The tribunal generally cannot compel privileged disclosures

Even in the more relaxed evidentiary environment of tribunals, privilege
retains its full protection.
