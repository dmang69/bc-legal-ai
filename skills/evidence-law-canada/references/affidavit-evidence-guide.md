# Affidavit Evidence — Practical Guide

Affidavit evidence has its own rules. What you can put in an affidavit,
how it's assessed, and what the traps are.

---

## The Fundamentals

An affidavit is:
- A written statement
- Of facts
- Sworn or affirmed
- Before a person authorized to administer oaths

Everything in an affidavit is offered as evidence. Everything is subject
to cross-examination if cross-examination is available.

---

## What Can Go In an Affidavit

### Personal Knowledge Statements

The default. The affiant states what they personally know.

Signals of personal knowledge:
- "I saw / heard / did / said"
- "On [date], I was present at..."
- "I signed / received / sent..."

Weight: strong.

### Information and Belief

Where the affiant learned facts from someone else and believes them
true. Must be clearly identified.

Format:
- "I am informed by [source] and verily believe that [fact]"
- "Based on information received from [source]..."

The source must be identifiable — "I am informed by a reliable source"
without identification is often disregarded.

Weight: weaker than knowledge, sometimes admissible only for
interlocutory matters.

### Documentary Exhibits

Documents attached as exhibits become part of the evidence when the
affiant properly identifies them.

Standard form:
"Attached and marked as Exhibit "A" to this affidavit is a true copy of
[description of document]."

Common exhibit types:
- Correspondence (letters, emails)
- Contracts and agreements
- Prior filings or decisions
- Photographs
- Records

Each exhibit needs to be identified in the body of the affidavit and
attached in proper form.

## What CANNOT Go In an Affidavit (Or Should Not)

### Argument

Affidavits contain evidence. Submissions contain argument. Mixing them
weakens both.

Wrong: "The landlord clearly intended to circumvent the RTA."
Better: state the facts. Save the characterization for submissions.

### Legal Conclusions

Affiants generally cannot swear to legal conclusions.

Wrong: "The notice was not properly served."
Better: state the facts about how the notice was received. Whether
service was "proper" is a legal question.

### Characterizations of Motive

Unless the affiant has direct knowledge of another person's motive
(rare), motive characterizations are opinion, not fact.

Wrong: "The landlord served the notice because they wanted to raise
the rent."
Better: state the facts (timeline of notice, subsequent re-rental,
rental rate) and let the tribunal draw the inference.

### Hearsay Not Properly Framed

Repeating what someone else said, offered for its truth, without proper
information-and-belief framing.

Wrong: "The tenant next door said the landlord was renovating."
Better: "I am informed by [Name] and verily believe that [fact]." Or
attach an affidavit from the tenant next door.

### Speculation

Guesses about what someone else knew, thought, or intended.

Wrong: "I believe the landlord must have known..."
Better: state observable facts.

### Argumentative Descriptions

Loaded language that presumes the conclusion.

Wrong: "The landlord's manipulative scheme..."
Better: "On [date], the landlord did [specific action]."

## Structural Requirements

### Standard Opening

Most jurisdictions require an opening substantially in the form:

"I, [full name], of [address], [occupation], SWEAR / AFFIRM AS FOLLOWS:"

Followed by:

"1. I have personal knowledge of the matters and facts hereinafter
deposed to except where stated to be on information and belief and,
where so stated, I do verily believe them to be true."

### Numbered Paragraphs

Each fact in its own paragraph. Numbered sequentially. Short paragraphs
better than long ones.

### Present or Past Tense

Facts are stated in the tense they occurred.

### Chronological Structure

Usually easiest to follow. Not required.

### Jurat

The concluding section where the affidavit is sworn. Includes:
- Location
- Date
- Signature of affiant
- Signature of commissioner
- Commissioner's identification

## Exhibits — Practical Guidance

### Numbering / Lettering

Exhibits are typically lettered (A, B, C...) or numbered (1, 2, 3...).
Whichever you use, use consistently.

### Cross-Referencing

Every exhibit must be referred to in the body of the affidavit. The
reference identifies the document and its relevance.

### Physical Attachment

Each exhibit needs to be:
- Physically or electronically attached
- Marked with the exhibit identifier
- Endorsed by the commissioner (or per applicable rules)

### Redaction

If exhibiting a document that contains privileged, irrelevant, or
sensitive material, redact appropriately and note the redaction.

### Bulk Documents

Where many documents are exhibited, consider an index or bundle. Some
jurisdictions require it above a threshold.

## Sworn vs Affirmed

Same legal effect. Distinction is one of religious/personal preference:

- **Sworn** — on the affiant's religious book (traditionally Bible;
  now any religious text or none)
- **Affirmed** — solemn declaration without religious reference

Both attract the same penalties for false statements (perjury).

## Cross-Examination on Affidavit

In some proceedings, an affiant may be cross-examined on their affidavit.

Preparation for cross:

```
□ Read your own affidavit three times before cross
□ Have supporting documents at hand
□ Know every statement — every source, every basis
□ Anticipate challenges to each paragraph
□ Practice responding to hostile questions
□ Do not exceed the affidavit — if you don't know, say so
```

Preparation to cross others:

```
□ Read the opposing affidavit using critical-reading protocols
□ Sort every statement into knowledge / information-and-belief /
   argument / conclusion
□ Identify contradictions with documents or other evidence
□ Identify unsourced statements
□ Identify argumentative content that can be attacked as improper
□ Prepare specific questions on each load-bearing paragraph
```

## The Motion to Strike

Some jurisdictions permit motions to strike improper affidavit content.
Even where formal motions are not available, tribunals may give reduced
weight to improper content on request.

Grounds:

- Statements not based on personal knowledge or proper information and
  belief
- Argument masquerading as evidence
- Legal conclusions
- Irrelevant material
- Speculation
- Scandalous or vexatious material

The remedy may be strike, redaction, or reduced weight.

## Affidavit in Judicial Review

Special considerations:

- **No new evidence rule** — generally, only evidence before the
  tribunal can be considered on judicial review, subject to narrow
  exceptions (procedural fairness, jurisdictional facts, background)
- **The record** — the affidavit typically annexes the tribunal record
  rather than re-litigating the merits
- **Petitioner's affidavit** — identifies the petitioner, establishes
  standing, may include limited procedural fairness evidence
- **Respondent's affidavit** — often unnecessary if the record is
  complete

Getting the affidavit wrong in JR can attract objection and reduce the
strength of the petition.

---

## Common Failures — Self-Represented Litigants

Common mistakes:

- Treating the affidavit as argument
- Mixing knowledge and belief without distinction
- Including hearsay without framing
- Attaching exhibits without proper reference or endorsement
- Long, argumentative paragraphs
- Emotional or characterizing language
- Failing to organize chronologically or by issue

Discipline: state the facts. State them precisely. Let submissions do
the argumentative work. An affidavit that is disciplined earns
credibility; one that is not, loses it.
