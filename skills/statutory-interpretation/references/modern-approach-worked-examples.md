# Modern Approach — Worked Examples

Applied to RTA and general interpretation questions.

---

## Example 1 — Bad Faith under RTA s.51

**Question:** What does "in good faith" require under RTA s.51 for a
landlord ending a tenancy to occupy the unit?

### TEXT

The phrase "in good faith" is not defined in the RTA. In ordinary
usage, "good faith" means honestly, without ulterior motive.

### CONTEXT

- RTA s.51 sits in Part 4 of the Act, addressing ending tenancies.
- Adjacent provisions require notice, compensation, and specific
  grounds.
- RTA s.51.4 imposes obligations to actually occupy and consequences
  for failure.
- The scheme suggests good faith is a live requirement, not a formality.

### PURPOSE

The RTA is remedial legislation protecting tenants and regulating
tenancies. Purposes include stability of tenure and prevention of
disguised evictions for other purposes (rent increases, resale, etc.).

Good faith under s.51 must serve this purpose. A landlord who intends
to use s.51 as a rent-raising mechanism is not acting in good faith.

### HARMONY

The interpretation of "good faith" that harmonizes text, context, and
purpose is:

An honest intention at the time of service to occupy the unit for the
stated purpose, without ulterior motive.

Consistent with:
- Text (ordinary meaning of good faith)
- Context (compensation regime under s.51.4)
- Purpose (protecting against disguised evictions)

Supported by the framework in *Gichuru v Palmar Properties* and RTB
Policy Guideline 2A.

## Example 2 — "Occupant" vs "Tenant" under the RTA

**Question:** When someone lives in a unit but is not on the tenancy
agreement, are they an "occupant" or a "tenant" under the RTA?

### TEXT

RTA definitions (s.1):
- "Tenant" — someone with rights and obligations under a tenancy
  agreement
- "Occupant" — a person, other than a tenant, who occupies a rental
  unit

The distinction is between tenancy-agreement holders and non-parties
who reside in the unit.

### CONTEXT

- Multiple RTA provisions distinguish tenants from occupants
- Different rights and remedies attach
- A person may become a tenant through conduct (implied tenancy) even
  without a written agreement

### PURPOSE

The RTA protects those with tenancy rights and provides different
treatment for those without them. The distinction matters for eviction
procedures, notice requirements, and other protections.

### HARMONY

The correct approach:

- Look at the actual arrangement, not the label used
- If a person pays rent, has exclusive possession, and has ongoing
  occupancy under an arrangement with the landlord (even oral), they
  may be a tenant
- If they merely reside as guests or family members without such
  arrangement, they are occupants

Substance over form (the equitable maxim) applies. The RTA's remedial
purpose supports finding tenancy where the arrangement functions as
one, even without formal documentation.

## Example 3 — Silence in a Statute

**Question:** The RTA specifies certain grounds for ending a tenancy.
May a landlord end a tenancy on grounds not specified in the RTA?

### TEXT

RTA Part 4 lists grounds for ending tenancies. The list is detailed
and specific.

### CONTEXT

- The Act's scheme is exhaustive on this point
- RTA s.5 provides the Act applies despite any agreement to the contrary
- The scheme suggests the Act's grounds are the only grounds

### PURPOSE

Protecting tenants against arbitrary eviction is central to the RTA.
Allowing landlords to invoke unenumerated grounds would defeat this
purpose.

### INTERPRETIVE RULES APPLIED

- **Expressio unius est exclusio alterius** — the express listing of
  grounds implies exclusion of others
- **Purpose-driven** — the remedial purpose reinforces the exclusion

### HARMONY

The interpretation: RTA Part 4 grounds are exhaustive. A landlord
cannot end a tenancy on grounds not listed.

Note: this is a strong case for expressio unius because the statutory
scheme is systematic. In other statutes, the same rule might not apply
where the listing is illustrative rather than exhaustive.

## Example 4 — Ambiguity Between Text and Purpose

**Question:** [Hypothetical] A statute uses the word "vehicle" without
definition. A dispute arises whether "vehicle" includes bicycles.

### TEXT

Ordinary meaning of "vehicle" — likely includes motorized road vehicles
and often bicycles too. Ambiguous at the margin.

### CONTEXT

The statute in question is a Motor Vehicle Act. Context suggests
"vehicle" in this statute refers to motor vehicles.

### PURPOSE

The Act's purpose is regulating motor vehicle use.

### HARMONY

Text is ambiguous. Context and purpose point strongly to motor vehicles
only. The correct interpretation excludes bicycles.

This example shows how context and purpose can narrow textually broad
language.

## Example 5 — Absurdity Avoidance

**Question:** [Hypothetical] A regulation says "no dog shall enter the
park." A guide dog assisting a person with a disability enters the
park. Does the regulation exclude the guide dog?

### TEXT

Literal text: no dog. Guide dogs are dogs.

### ABSURDITY

Excluding a guide dog would prevent a person with a disability from
using the park — an absurd result inconsistent with human rights
legislation and reasonable regulatory purpose.

### PRESUMPTIONS

- Presumption against absurdity
- Presumption of constitutionality (Charter s.15 implications)
- Presumption of compliance with human rights law

### HARMONY

The interpretation: "dog" is read purposively to exclude guide dogs
and service animals. The literal text yields to the harmonious reading.

## Example 6 — Applying the Modern Approach to an ATA s.58 JR

**Question:** The RTB arbitrator interpreted RTA s.[X] in a particular
way. Was that interpretation patently unreasonable?

### PROCESS

Step 1 — State the modern approach as the governing framework.

Step 2 — Analyze the correct interpretation using text, context, and
purpose.

Step 3 — Compare the arbitrator's interpretation to the correct
interpretation.

Step 4 — Assess whether the arbitrator's interpretation could
reasonably result from the modern approach.

Step 5 — If not, argue patent unreasonableness.

### KEY POINT

The bar is not "the arbitrator got it wrong." The bar is "no reasonable
interpretation could reach the arbitrator's result." This is the
patent unreasonableness threshold.

For a merely wrong interpretation (that a reasonable interpreter could
have reached), the JR fails. For an interpretation that ignores text,
context, or purpose in a fundamental way, the JR may succeed.

## Discipline in Interpretation Arguments

Common failures:

- **Text-only argument** — ignoring context and purpose weakens the
  argument
- **Purpose-only argument** — reading against text without acknowledging
  the text is a stretch
- **Ignoring the modern approach framing** — courts expect the modern
  approach to be the frame
- **Rule-collecting** — throwing every Latin maxim without integration
- **Assumption of correctness** — treating your interpretation as
  obvious rather than earning it through analysis

The strongest interpretation arguments:

- State the modern approach explicitly
- Work through text, context, and purpose systematically
- Show harmony among all three
- Address the opposing interpretation and explain why it fails one or
  more elements
- Cite supporting authorities where available

An interpretation argument that reads like an application of the modern
approach is much stronger than one that assumes the answer.
