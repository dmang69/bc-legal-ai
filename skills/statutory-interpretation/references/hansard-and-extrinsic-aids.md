# Hansard and Extrinsic Aids

Sources beyond the statute itself, and how to use them.

---

## The Basic Position

Extrinsic aids are admissible in Canadian statutory interpretation.
The rule against consulting legislative history has been substantially
relaxed since *Rizzo & Rizzo Shoes* 1998 SCC.

But extrinsic aids are not equal in weight. Knowing which sources carry
weight, and how much, matters.

---

## Hansard / Legislative Debates

### What It Is

The official record of parliamentary or legislative debate. Includes
speeches, questions, and comments during the passage of the bill.

### How to Access

- **Federal Hansard** — available online at parl.ca (House of Commons
  and Senate)
- **BC Hansard** — available at leg.bc.ca (Hansard Services)
- Similar resources for other provinces
- Historical Hansard may require library access

### What Carries Weight

**Higher weight:**
- Statements by the sponsoring minister or bill's sponsor
- Statements specifically addressing the provision at issue
- Statements addressing the purpose of the bill
- Statements not contradicted in subsequent debate
- Committee reports on the bill

**Lower weight:**
- Statements by opposition members (they didn't draft the bill)
- Broad political statements not addressing the specific provision
- Statements that fail to address the interpretive question
- Statements contradicted by other members

### The Traditional Cautions

Even under the modern relaxed approach, courts note:

- Legislative history is one input, not decisive
- Individual statements may not reflect legislative intent
- Ambiguity in debate doesn't necessarily resolve ambiguity in statute
- Reliance on Hansard should be transparent

### The Argument Technique

If arguing based on Hansard:

1. Cite the specific Hansard reference (date, page/column)
2. Quote the relevant passage
3. Identify the speaker (was this the sponsor?)
4. Explain why this statement bears on the interpretation
5. Address whether the statement was contradicted or qualified

Bad Hansard argument: "In debate, [opposition MP] said X, so the
provision means X."

Better Hansard argument: "The Minister of [X], introducing the bill,
stated at [reference] that the provision was designed to [purpose].
This is consistent with the purposive interpretation urged here."

## Committee Reports

### What They Are

Reports from legislative committees studying the bill. May include:

- Background research
- Explanations of specific provisions
- Amendments proposed
- Reasons for or against amendments
- Testimony from witnesses

### Weight

Often more detailed and considered than debates. Weight can be
significant where the committee report is contemporaneous with the bill
and addresses the specific issue.

Committee reports from bodies of experts (e.g. Law Reform Commissions)
whose recommendations were adopted also carry weight.

## Prior Enactments

### The Concept

Where a statute replaces or amends earlier legislation, comparing the
old text to the new can illuminate.

- If the change is dramatic, purpose likely differs
- If wording is preserved, prior case law interpreting that wording is
  presumptively still relevant
- If wording is changed subtly, the legislature may have intended a
  specific change

### The Argument Technique

- Identify the predecessor provision
- Show the specific change
- Consider what that change signals about legislative intent

### Cautions

- Not every change signals a substantive shift — some are cosmetic
- The absence of a change is also informative (silence may be
  intentional)

## Related Legislation

### Statutes on the Same Subject

Where multiple statutes address related subjects, they may be read
together. Examples:

- RTA read with Manufactured Home Park Tenancy Act
- ATA read with individual tribunal governing statutes
- Provincial Interpretation Act applied to any provincial statute

### The In Pari Materia Rule

Statutes dealing with the same subject matter are to be read together
(*in pari materia*). Consistent interpretation across related statutes
is preferred.

### Cautions

- The connection must be genuine, not merely thematic
- Amendments to one statute don't automatically affect another
- Distinct legislative purposes may still support distinct
  interpretations

## International Agreements

### When Relevant

Where a statute:

- Implements an international agreement
- Is designed to give effect to an international obligation
- Addresses subject matter also covered by international agreements
  Canada has entered

...the agreement may be an interpretive aid.

### Weight

- Higher for statutes explicitly implementing agreements
- Lower for statutes tangentially touching international obligations

### Application

For federal statutes implementing treaties, the treaty text often
governs. For provincial statutes, the connection may be less direct.

## Case Law Interpreting Predecessor Legislation

### The Rule

Case law interpreting predecessor provisions may inform current
interpretation, particularly where the current provision preserves the
prior language.

### The Legislative Response

Where the legislature has amended a provision after a court's
interpretation:

- If the amendment adopts the interpretation, it is codified
- If the amendment changes the wording, the legislature may have
  intended to displace the interpretation
- If the amendment leaves the provision untouched despite the
  interpretation, the interpretation is often reinforced

### Cautions

- Case law becomes less relevant as statutory language diverges
- The framework of interpretation may have shifted (e.g. pre-Rizzo
  cases interpret under different principles)

## Academic Commentary

### When Cited

Textbooks and articles may be cited to support an interpretation.

Canadian authorities frequently cited for interpretation:
- Ruth Sullivan, *The Construction of Statutes* (widely regarded as
  the leading text)
- Pierre-André Côté, *The Interpretation of Legislation in Canada*
- Elmer Driedger, *The Construction of Statutes* (the classic
  original)

### Weight

Depends on:
- Authority of the author
- Whether the work addresses the specific question
- Whether the court has cited it in similar contexts

### Cautions

Academic commentary is persuasive, not binding. It complements but
does not replace analysis of text, context, and purpose.

---

## Regulatory Impact Analysis Statements

For federal regulations, Regulatory Impact Analysis Statements (RIAS)
are published in the Canada Gazette and explain the regulatory intent.

RIAS can be used to identify the purpose of regulations.

## Practical Advice

### Where to Start

For most interpretation questions, the modern approach analysis (text,
context, purpose) will resolve the question. Extrinsic aids are:

- A supplement, not a substitute
- Useful where genuine ambiguity remains
- Persuasive where the aid clearly addresses the interpretive question

### When Extrinsic Aids Are Most Useful

- The provision is genuinely ambiguous
- The context and purpose don't fully resolve the question
- The extrinsic source directly addresses the interpretive point
- The source is authoritative (Minister's statement, committee report)

### When Extrinsic Aids Add Little

- The text is clear
- The extrinsic source is silent or ambiguous
- The source is from someone whose views weren't determinative
- The court is likely to resolve the question on text, context, and
  purpose alone

### The Argument Structure

If using extrinsic aids:

1. Complete the modern approach analysis first
2. Note the residual ambiguity or uncertainty
3. Introduce the extrinsic aid as supplementary
4. Explain what it adds
5. Reach the interpretation

Do not lead with extrinsic aids. Do not treat them as decisive. Use
them as they should be used — to inform, not to control.
