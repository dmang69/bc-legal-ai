# Contract Elements Checklist

Element-by-element checklists for contract formation, breach, and
defences. Use to structure a contract claim or defence.

---

## Contract Formation

```
□ 1. OFFER
     Manifestation of willingness to enter into contract on specified terms
     Distinguished from invitation to treat (advertisements, displays)
     Distinguished from mere puff

□ 2. ACCEPTANCE
     Unqualified assent to the offer
     Communicated (except in narrow exceptions like unilateral contracts)
     Mirror image rule: acceptance must match offer; variation is a
     counter-offer (which rejects the original)

□ 3. CONSIDERATION
     Something of value exchanged for the promise
     Must move from the promisee
     Must not be past consideration
     Must be sufficient (not adequate — courts do not weigh adequacy)
     Nominal consideration generally sufficient (peppercorn principle)

□ 4. INTENTION to CREATE LEGAL RELATIONS
     Presumed in commercial contexts
     Presumed absent in social/family contexts (rebuttable)

□ 5. CAPACITY
     Minors: contracts voidable at their option (with exceptions for
     necessaries)
     Mental incapacity: contracts voidable if other party knew
     Corporations: within the powers of the corporate constitution

□ 6. LEGALITY
     Subject matter must not be illegal or contrary to public policy
     Illegality may make contract void or unenforceable
```

---

## Breach

```
□ 1. VALID CONTRACT existed
     All formation elements met

□ 2. OBLIGATION under contract
     Identify the specific term breached
     Distinguish express and implied terms
     Distinguish conditions, warranties, and innominate terms

□ 3. BREACH occurred
     Failure to perform the obligation
     May be actual (at time of performance) or anticipatory (advance
     manifestation of non-performance)

□ 4. NO EXCUSE for the breach
     Not excused by frustration, mistake, force majeure, or other
     recognized doctrine

□ 5. LOSS to plaintiff
     Actual loss caused by the breach (or nominal damages available)
```

---

## Types of Terms

**Condition** — a fundamental term of the contract. Breach entitles the
innocent party to treat the contract as repudiated AND claim damages.

**Warranty** — a subsidiary term. Breach entitles the innocent party to
damages only, not repudiation.

**Innominate term** — a term not classified in advance as condition or
warranty. Consequences of breach depend on the seriousness of the breach
(*Hongkong Fir* test).

---

## Damages for Breach

```
□ 1. LOSS actually suffered
□ 2. CAUSED by the breach (but-for)
□ 3. NOT TOO REMOTE (Hadley v Baxendale)
     - Arising naturally from the breach, OR
     - In the reasonable contemplation of the parties at time of contract
□ 4. MITIGATED
     Innocent party must take reasonable steps to reduce loss
     Damages for unmitigated loss are not recoverable
□ 5. CAN BE MEASURED
     Expectation measure (put in position as if contract performed)
     Reliance measure (put back to pre-contract position) — alternative
     Restitution measure — in limited cases
```

## Defences to Contract Claims

### Misrepresentation

```
□ 1. STATEMENT OF FACT (not opinion, not future intention)
□ 2. FALSE
□ 3. INDUCED entry into the contract
□ 4. Type:
     Fraudulent (Derry v Peek — knowledge, recklessness)
     Negligent (Hedley Byrne principles)
     Innocent (no fault)
□ 5. Remedies vary by type
     Rescission generally available for all types (subject to bars)
     Damages: available in tort for fraudulent and negligent
```

### Mistake

```
□ 1. MISTAKE as to something fundamental
□ 2. TYPE:
     Common mistake (both parties mistaken about same thing)
     Mutual mistake (parties at cross-purposes)
     Unilateral mistake (one party mistaken; other may or may not know)
□ 3. NARROW doctrine — courts are cautious to unwind bargains for mistake
```

### Duress

```
□ 1. PRESSURE applied to enter contract
□ 2. Type:
     Duress to the person (physical threat) — narrow but powerful
     Duress of goods (threat to property)
     Economic duress — the most common in commercial contexts
□ 3. ILLEGITIMATE pressure
□ 4. Sufficient to overbear the will (or leave no realistic alternative)
□ 5. CAUSED the entry into the contract
```

### Undue Influence

```
□ 1. RELATIONSHIP of influence
     Presumed in certain relationships (fiduciary, professional)
     May be proved in others
□ 2. TRANSACTION calling for explanation
     One that a person not under influence would not ordinarily enter
□ 3. UNDUE INFLUENCE inferred (rebuttable — e.g. by independent legal
     advice)
```

### Unconscionability

```
□ 1. INEQUALITY OF BARGAINING POWER
     Structural (e.g. standard form) or personal (e.g. distress)
□ 2. IMPROVIDENT BARGAIN
     Substantially unfair to the weaker party
□ 3. See Uber Technologies v Heller 2020 SCC — restated modern test
```

### Frustration

```
□ 1. SUPERVENING EVENT after contract formed
□ 2. NOT FORESEEN by parties
□ 3. RADICALLY CHANGES the obligations
□ 4. WITHOUT FAULT of the party invoking frustration
□ 5. Automatically discharges contract from date of frustrating event
□ 6. FLA (Frustrated Contract Act) may govern financial adjustments
```

### Illegality

```
□ 1. Contract or performance ILLEGAL by:
     - Statute (e.g. licensing regime)
     - Common law (contrary to public policy)
□ 2. Consequence:
     Void, unenforceable, or partially enforceable depending on the type
□ 3. Ex turpi causa — no cause of action arises from an illegal act
```

## Contract Interpretation Principles

When the contract exists but its meaning is disputed:

```
□ TEXT: Ordinary meaning of the words
□ CONTEXT: The whole contract read together
□ COMMERCIAL PURPOSE: Interpretation that makes commercial sense
□ FACTUAL MATRIX: Objective background reasonably available to both parties
     at the time of contract (Sattva v Creston 2014 SCC)
□ CONTRA PROFERENTEM: Ambiguity resolved against the drafter (last resort)
□ PAROL EVIDENCE RULE: Extrinsic evidence generally not admissible to
     contradict a fully integrated written agreement (with exceptions)
```

---

## Using the Checklists

For contract disputes:

1. Formation: verify all six elements present
2. Terms: identify the specific term at issue and its type
3. Breach: verify all four elements
4. Damages: run through the five-step damages checklist
5. Defences: run through each defence to check applicability

For opposing contract claims, absence of any element in the plaintiff's
pleading is potential attack surface.
