# Equity Doctrines — Expanded

Equitable doctrines still in active use in Canadian practice.

---

## The Equitable Maxims

Historical guiding principles. Not rules with binding force but
frameworks for the exercise of equitable discretion.

**"Equity looks to substance rather than form."**
Equity examines the reality of a transaction, not just its formal
description. A transaction called a sale but functioning as a loan may
be treated as a loan.

**"Equity will not suffer a wrong without a remedy."**
Where the common law provides no adequate remedy, equity may fashion
one. Basis for many equitable remedies.

**"He who seeks equity must do equity."**
A party seeking equitable relief must be prepared to act equitably
themselves. Cannot claim relief while withholding what is due.

**"He who comes to equity must come with clean hands."**
A party whose own conduct is tainted in relation to the matter cannot
obtain equitable relief. Narrow — the misconduct must relate to the
transaction, not merely the party's general character.

**"Delay defeats equity."** (Laches)
Unreasonable delay in seeking equitable relief may bar the claim.

**"Equity acts in personam."**
Equitable remedies operate against the person, not the property. This
distinction has procedural consequences.

**"Equity looks upon that as done which ought to be done."**
Where a person is obliged to perform an act, equity treats the act as
if done. Basis for constructive trusts in some contexts.

**"Where equities are equal, the law prevails."**
When neither party's equitable claim is stronger, the outcome depends
on legal rights.

**"Where equities are equal, the first in time prevails."**
Between equal equitable claims, priority is determined by time.

## Fiduciary Duties

A fiduciary duty is a duty of loyalty and utmost good faith owed by
one party (the fiduciary) to another (the beneficiary).

### Recognized Fiduciary Relationships

Presumed fiduciary:
- Trustee to beneficiary
- Solicitor to client
- Director to corporation
- Guardian to ward
- Agent to principal

Ad hoc fiduciary (must be proved):
- Doctor to patient (in specific circumstances)
- Business partner to partner (sometimes)
- Parent to child (specific contexts)
- Anyone in a relationship where the beneficiary is peculiarly vulnerable
  and the fiduciary has undertaken to act in their interest

### The Ad Hoc Fiduciary Test (post-Alberta v Elder Advocates)

1. An undertaking by the fiduciary to act in the best interests of
   the beneficiary
2. A defined person or class of persons vulnerable to the fiduciary
3. A legal or substantial practical interest of the beneficiary that
   stands to be adversely affected

### Fiduciary Obligations

- Loyalty (avoid conflicts of interest)
- Good faith
- Duty to act in the beneficiary's best interest
- Duty not to profit from the position without informed consent
- Duty to disclose material information

### Consequences of Breach

- Constructive trust over profits
- Account of profits
- Rescission of transactions
- Damages / equitable compensation

## Trusts

### Express Trust — Three Certainties

For a valid express trust, the settlor must establish:

1. **Certainty of intention** — clear intention to create a trust
2. **Certainty of subject-matter** — the trust property is identifiable
3. **Certainty of objects** — the beneficiaries are identifiable

Without any of these, the intended trust fails.

### Resulting Trust

Arises where legal title has been transferred but the beneficial
interest is presumed to remain with the transferor. Common triggers:

- Gratuitous transfer (though presumption of advancement may apply
  between certain family relationships)
- Purchase money resulting trust (A pays, B takes title)
- Failure of an express trust for uncertainty of objects

### Constructive Trust

An equitable remedy imposed to prevent unjust enrichment or the
retention of property acquired through breach of fiduciary duty.

Categories:
- Institutional constructive trust (arising from a rule of substantive
  law)
- Remedial constructive trust (imposed by the court as a remedy)

The Canadian approach recognizes both. See *Soulos v Korkontzilas*
1997 SCC.

### Trustee's Duties

- Follow the terms of the trust
- Act in the best interests of beneficiaries
- Loyalty (no conflicts, no profit)
- Prudent management of trust property
- Even-handedness among beneficiaries
- Duty to account

## Equitable Remedies

### Specific Performance

An order requiring a party to perform a contractual obligation.
Discretionary. Available where:

- Damages are inadequate
- The subject matter is unique (land is presumptively unique)
- The plaintiff is ready, willing, and able to perform their own
  obligations
- No bar to equitable relief (delay, unclean hands, hardship)

Rarely ordered for personal service contracts (constitutional / practical
concerns).

### Injunction

An order requiring conduct (mandatory injunction) or restraining conduct
(prohibitory injunction). May be:

- Perpetual (final)
- Interlocutory (until trial or further order)
- Interim (short-term, typically until inter partes hearing)

Interlocutory injunctions governed by the *RJR-MacDonald* test:

1. Serious issue to be tried
2. Irreparable harm to the applicant if injunction not granted
3. Balance of convenience favours granting the injunction

### Rescission

Setting aside a contract, restoring the parties to their pre-contract
positions. Available for misrepresentation, mistake, duress, undue
influence, and unconscionability.

Bars to rescission:
- Affirmation (electing to keep the contract with knowledge of the ground)
- Delay
- Restitutio in integrum impossible (parties cannot be restored)
- Third-party rights affected

### Rectification

Correcting a written document that does not reflect the parties' actual
agreement. Requires clear and convincing evidence of a common intention
inconsistent with the document.

Restrictive test post-*Canada (Attorney General) v Fairmont Hotels*
2016 SCC — retreat from broader use of rectification as a tax planning
tool.

### Account of Profits

Ordering a defendant to account for and disgorge profits obtained through
wrongdoing (typically breach of fiduciary duty or intellectual property
infringement).

### Equitable Compensation

Monetary compensation for equitable wrongs, calculated on equitable
principles rather than tort damages principles. Available for breach of
fiduciary duty and breach of trust.

## Tracing

The equitable process of identifying and following value through changes
in form or hands. Distinct from following (identifying the same property)
and claiming (asserting a right in what is traced).

Tracing may be:

- Through mixed funds (e.g. into a bank account containing other money)
- Through changes in form (money → asset → other asset)
- Into the hands of third parties (subject to bona fide purchaser defence)

Post-*Foskett v McKeown* [2001] UKHL, tracing is a process, not a remedy —
the plaintiff must then claim a remedy (constructive trust, equitable lien,
personal claim) against the traced property.

## Estoppel (Equitable Variants)

### Proprietary Estoppel

Where A has, by words or conduct, encouraged B to believe B has or will
have an interest in A's property, and B has acted in reliance to their
detriment, equity may enforce the expectation.

Elements:
- Assurance (express or by conduct)
- Reliance
- Detriment
- Unconscionability of denying the interest

### Promissory Estoppel

Where A has, by promise, encouraged B to believe A will not enforce a
strict legal right, and B has acted in reliance, A may be estopped from
enforcing the right (at least temporarily).

Requires:
- Clear promise
- Reliance
- Inequity in resiling

Traditional English rule: promissory estoppel is a "shield, not a sword"
— cannot found a cause of action, only defeat one. Canadian position
allows it more flexibly in some contexts.

---

## Laches

Unreasonable delay in seeking equitable relief, combined with prejudice
to the defendant, may bar the claim.

Requires more than delay alone — the delay must be prejudicial. Statutory
limitation periods now largely displace laches but the doctrine survives
where limitation statutes don't apply.

---

## Notice

The doctrine determining when a person is affected by an equitable
interest they don't have actual knowledge of.

- Actual notice — real knowledge
- Constructive notice — knowledge a person would have if they made
  reasonable inquiries
- Imputed notice — notice held by an agent, attributed to the principal

Modern registration systems (e.g. BC Land Title System for real property)
largely displace common-law notice rules for registered interests.
