# Tort Elements Checklist

Element-by-element checklists for common torts. Use these to structure a
claim or to identify what's missing from an opposing party's pleading.

---

## Negligence

```
□ 1. DUTY OF CARE OWED
     Is the plaintiff a "neighbour" in the Donoghue v Stevenson sense?
     Test: Anns/Cooper — foreseeability + proximity + no policy reason to
     negate at stage 2

□ 2. BREACH OF STANDARD OF CARE
     Did the defendant fall below the standard of a reasonable person in
     their position?
     Considerations: probability of harm, seriousness of harm, cost of
     precaution, social utility

□ 3. FACTUAL CAUSATION
     "But for" the defendant's breach, would the plaintiff have suffered
     the harm? (Athey v Leonati)
     Material contribution test applies where "but-for" is impossible
     (Clements v Clements — narrow)

□ 4. LEGAL CAUSATION (REMOTENESS)
     Was the harm reasonably foreseeable as a consequence of the breach?
     (Mustapha v Culligan)

□ 5. ACTUAL DAMAGE
     Has the plaintiff suffered compensable damage? (Not merely fear of
     future harm, absent Mustapha threshold)

DEFENCES TO CONSIDER
□ Contributory negligence (statutory apportionment)
□ Voluntary assumption of risk (volenti — narrow)
□ Illegality (ex turpi causa)
□ Limitation period expired
```

## Nuisance (Private)

```
□ 1. INTERFERENCE with plaintiff's use or enjoyment of land
     May be physical damage or intangible interference (noise, odour,
     vibration)

□ 2. UNREASONABLE interference
     Balance: character of neighbourhood, sensitivity of plaintiff,
     duration and frequency, utility of defendant's conduct

□ 3. PLAINTIFF has interest in land affected
     Ownership, tenancy, or exclusive occupation

□ 4. CAUSATION and DAMAGE
     Interference actually caused loss, discomfort, or damage

DEFENCES
□ Statutory authority (narrow — must show inevitability)
□ Prescription (20 years, rare in modern practice)
□ Consent
```

## Trespass to Land

```
□ 1. DIRECT ENTRY onto plaintiff's land
     Or placing / causing an object to enter
     Direct entry distinguished from consequential (which sounds in
     nuisance or negligence)

□ 2. INTENTIONAL or NEGLIGENT
     Intentional includes reckless

□ 3. WITHOUT LAWFUL JUSTIFICATION
     E.g. no licence, no statutory authority, no necessity

Note: trespass is actionable per se — no proof of damage required

DEFENCES
□ Consent (licence)
□ Statutory authority
□ Necessity (narrow)
□ Legal justification (e.g. police entry under warrant)
```

## Battery

```
□ 1. DIRECT PHYSICAL CONTACT with plaintiff
     Or setting in motion a force that results in contact

□ 2. INTENTIONAL
     Intention to make contact, not necessarily to cause harm

□ 3. WITHOUT CONSENT
     Consent may be express or implied by context (e.g. normal contacts
     of daily life)

Note: actionable per se

DEFENCES
□ Consent
□ Self-defence (proportionate)
□ Defence of others / property
□ Necessity
□ Legal authority (e.g. lawful arrest)
```

---

## Assault (Civil)

```
□ 1. ACT creating REASONABLE APPREHENSION of imminent battery
     Words alone are generally insufficient; must be accompanied by conduct

□ 2. INTENTIONAL

□ 3. APPARENT ABILITY to carry through (need not be actual)

Note: no contact required — that's battery

DEFENCES
□ Consent
□ Legal authority
□ Self-defence
```

---

## False Imprisonment

```
□ 1. DIRECT and INTENTIONAL confinement
     Physical or by assertion of authority

□ 2. WITHOUT lawful authority

□ 3. TOTAL restraint (some reasonable means of escape need not be tried
     if not obvious)

Note: actionable per se — no proof of damage required

DEFENCES
□ Lawful authority (police, statutory)
□ Consent
□ Necessity
```

---

## Defamation

```
□ 1. DEFAMATORY MEANING
     Would lower the plaintiff in the estimation of reasonable persons
     Meaning is the natural and ordinary meaning, or a true innuendo

□ 2. REFERS TO PLAINTIFF
     Explicitly or by identifying description

□ 3. PUBLISHED to a third party
     Communication to at least one person other than the plaintiff

Note: falsity is presumed; defendant must prove truth

DEFENCES
□ Justification (truth)
□ Absolute privilege (parliamentary, judicial)
□ Qualified privilege (duty/interest, common interest, moral duty)
□ Fair comment
□ Responsible communication on matters of public interest (Grant v Torstar)
□ Consent
```

---

## Intentional Infliction of Mental Suffering

```
□ 1. FLAGRANT OR OUTRAGEOUS conduct
     Beyond mere insult or ordinary misconduct

□ 2. INTENTIONAL or reckless as to the psychiatric consequence
     Includes conduct calculated to produce mental suffering

□ 3. PSYCHIATRIC INJURY (a visible and provable illness)
     Not merely upset or ordinary distress

Note: high threshold; distinguish from ordinary emotional distress claims
```

## Occupier's Liability (BC — Occupiers Liability Act)

```
□ 1. DEFENDANT is an OCCUPIER of premises (statutory definition)

□ 2. PLAINTIFF was on premises

□ 3. DEFENDANT owed statutory DUTY to take reasonable care that the
     plaintiff will be reasonably safe in using the premises (s.3)

□ 4. BREACH of that duty

□ 5. CAUSATION and DAMAGE

MODIFICATIONS
□ For trespassers, willful/reckless conduct (s.3(3.3))
□ For recreational users of certain lands, willful/reckless (s.3.3)
□ Warning may satisfy duty in some circumstances
```

## Using the Checklists

For each tort claim you're building or opposing:

1. Copy the checklist for the relevant tort
2. Under each element, note the evidence supporting or negating it
3. Identify which elements are contested and which are conceded
4. For contested elements, plan how you'll prove or defeat them

For opposing pleadings, check whether every element has been pleaded. A
pleading missing an element is vulnerable to a strike application.
