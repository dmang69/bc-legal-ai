# Vavilov Framework — Extended Analysis

*Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v Vavilov* 2019 SCC 65
restated Canadian administrative law. The framework in detail.

---

## What Vavilov Changed

### Before Vavilov (Dunsmuir Framework)

*Dunsmuir v New Brunswick* 2008 SCC 9 established:
- Two standards: correctness and reasonableness
- Contextual factors for standard selection
- Presumption of reasonableness for interpretation of home statute
- "True jurisdictional questions" attracted correctness

### After Vavilov

- Reasonableness is the presumptive standard
- Five specific categories where correctness applies
- "True jurisdictional questions" eliminated as a separate category
- Reasonableness review focuses on the reasoning process, not just outcome
- Enhanced role for reasons in the analysis

---

## The Five Correctness Categories

### Category 1 — Constitutional Questions

Constitutional questions attract correctness. Examples:
- Division of powers
- Charter challenges
- Aboriginal rights under s.35
- Constitutional application of a statute

Rationale: constitutional interpretation is core judicial function.

### Category 2 — General Questions of Law of Central Importance

Questions of law of central importance to the legal system as a whole,
beyond the specific statutory context. Examples:
- Solicitor-client privilege doctrine
- Res judicata principles
- Interpretation of Charter provisions
- Fundamental principles of statutory interpretation

Rationale: uniformity in general law is a judicial function.

### Category 3 — Jurisdictional Boundaries Between Tribunals

Questions about which of two or more administrative bodies has
jurisdiction. Correctness ensures consistent line-drawing.

Not to be confused with the eliminated "true jurisdictional questions"
category — this is only about boundary questions between competing
tribunals.

### Category 4 — Statutory Rights of Appeal

Where the legislature has provided for an appeal to a court from an
administrative decision, questions of law on that appeal receive
correctness review (matching the appellate framework).

### Category 5 — Legislated Standards

Where the legislature has clearly indicated an intent for correctness
review, that intent controls. Rare, but possible.

## Reasonableness Review Post-Vavilov

### The Core Question

Is the decision transparent, intelligible, and justified?

### Two Constraints

**Legal constraints:**
- Applicable statutes and regulations
- Common law
- International law
- Constitutional norms
- Principles of statutory interpretation
- Past interpretations of the enabling statute

**Factual constraints:**
- The evidence in the record
- Findings of fact
- Submissions of the parties

An unreasonable decision is one that fails to justify itself in
relation to these constraints.

### The Role of Reasons

Reasons matter significantly under Vavilov:

- Reasons must engage with central submissions
- Reasons must show justification, not just conclusion
- Reasons must be intelligible to affected parties
- Absent or inadequate reasons may support a finding of
  unreasonableness

### What Counts as Unreasonable

Post-Vavilov, decisions may be unreasonable where they:

- Fail to grapple with central submissions
- Contain internal contradictions
- Ignore relevant law
- Misapply relevant law
- Reach outcomes not supported by the reasoning
- Are opaque about the reasoning process
- Ignore contextual constraints of the specific case

### What Is Not Unreasonable

- Interpretations the reviewing court disagrees with (if reasonable)
- Weight assessments (which are for the decision-maker)
- Choices among reasonable outcomes
- Reasons that are brief but adequate

---

## The Reasons Requirement

### When Reasons Are Required

Post-Vavilov guidance:
- Reasons are generally expected for significant decisions
- The degree of reasoning must match the stakes
- Reasons need not be perfect but must be responsive

### What Adequate Reasons Look Like

- Address the central issues
- Show the reasoning path from evidence to conclusion
- Engage with the parties' submissions
- Note the applicable law
- Explain any departure from prior decisions

### When Reasons Fail

- Boilerplate that could apply to any case
- Ignoring key submissions
- Skipping the analytical step
- Contradicting the record
- Unintelligibility

## The Statutory Standard Question

Where a statute prescribes a standard, that standard applies. In BC:

**Administrative Tribunals Act (ATA)** s.58 prescribes:
- Patent unreasonableness for findings of fact
- Patent unreasonableness for discretionary decisions
- Depending on the tribunal, patent unreasonableness or reasonableness
  for other questions

**Judicial Review Procedure Act (JRPA)** — provides the procedural
framework.

Post-Vavilov, statutory standards persist. Vavilov did not override
provincial statutory choices about deference.

---

## Applying Vavilov in an RTB JR

For a JR of an RTB decision under ATA s.58:

```
1. Identify the type of question in each ground:
   - Fact → s.58(2)(a) patent unreasonableness
   - Discretion → s.58(2)(a) patent unreasonableness
   - Interpretation of RTA → check specific ATA subsection
   - Procedural fairness → Baker analysis
   - Constitutional → correctness

2. For patent unreasonableness grounds:
   - Frame the argument to the very high threshold
   - Show the decision could not result from a reasonable process
   - Cite Speckling and other patent unreasonableness cases

3. For reasonableness grounds (if applicable):
   - Argue Vavilov reasonableness — attention to reasons
   - Show what central submissions were ignored
   - Show what legal constraints were missed
   - Show what factual constraints were violated

4. For procedural fairness grounds:
   - Apply the Baker factors
   - Identify the specific procedural defect
   - Show it affected the outcome (usually presumed for serious defects)
```

## The Culture Shift

Vavilov signaled a shift in tone:

- Deference is not blind endorsement
- Reasons must justify
- Reasonableness has teeth
- Consistency and predictability matter

For a JR petitioner, this means:

- Argue the reasoning of the impugned decision, not just the outcome
- Point to specific failures of justification
- Show what central submissions the tribunal ignored
- Cite Vavilov language where it fits the argument

For a respondent defending the decision:

- Explain the reasoning
- Address arguments the petitioner claims were ignored
- Show the decision falls within the range of reasonable outcomes
- Cite the deferential nature of reasonableness review

## Common Vavilov Arguments

### "The tribunal failed to grapple with our central submission"

Effective where the reasons ignore a key argument. Requires showing:
- What the submission was
- Where in the record it was raised
- That the reasons do not address it
- That it was material to the outcome

### "The interpretation is not one that could result from the modern
approach"

Effective for statutory interpretation grounds. Requires:
- Setting out the modern approach
- Showing what interpretation would result
- Showing the tribunal's interpretation fails one or more elements

### "The reasoning is internally inconsistent"

Effective where the tribunal contradicts itself or reaches a conclusion
that doesn't follow from its findings. Requires precise citation to the
specific inconsistencies.

### "The decision falls outside the range of reasonable outcomes"

The hardest argument to sustain. Requires showing that no reasonable
tribunal on this record could have reached this outcome — high bar.

## Vavilov and Patent Unreasonableness

Vavilov did not abolish patent unreasonableness — it remains where
legislation prescribes it. But Vavilov's culture shift may reach patent
unreasonableness too:

- Reasons matter under any standard
- Justification is required
- Ignoring central submissions is problematic
- Internal contradictions weaken decisions

Argument technique: cite Vavilov's culture shift while applying the
prescribed patent unreasonableness standard. The tribunal's failure to
provide justification may support the argument even where the standard
is patent unreasonableness.
