# Bias Doctrine — Detailed Guide

Bias analysis in Canadian administrative law.

---

## The Two Types

**Actual bias** — the decision-maker actually decided in advance or was
predisposed. Requires evidence of the decision-maker's actual mental
state. Rarely provable directly.

**Reasonable apprehension of bias** — an informed observer, thinking the
matter through, would perceive a real likelihood of bias. Does not
require proof of actual bias.

Reasonable apprehension is the more usual claim.

---

## The Foundational Test

*Committee for Justice and Liberty v National Energy Board* [1978] 1 SCR
369, per de Grandpré J:

The apprehension of bias must be a reasonable one, held by reasonable
and right-minded persons, applying themselves to the question and
obtaining thereon the required information. The test is:

> "What would an informed person, viewing the matter realistically and
> practically — and having thought the matter through — conclude?
> Would he think it is more likely than not that [the decision-maker],
> whether consciously or unconsciously, would not decide fairly?"

The key phrase: "more likely than not."

---

## The Wewaykum Refinement

*Wewaykum Indian Band v Canada* 2003 SCC 45 emphasized:

- The threshold is HIGH
- Judges and decision-makers are presumed impartial
- The party alleging bias bears the burden
- Cogent evidence is required
- Speculation is insufficient
- Adverse rulings alone are not evidence of bias

---

## Actual Bias — The Sources

Direct evidence of actual bias may include:

- Statements by the decision-maker showing predisposition
- Written communications indicating preconceived views
- Financial interest in the outcome
- Personal relationships with a party
- Prior involvement with the matter creating conflict

Actual bias, if proved, is disqualifying without further analysis.

---

## Reasonable Apprehension — The Sources

### Prior Involvement

- The decision-maker was previously involved in the matter (as
  investigator, counsel, prior adjudicator)
- The decision-maker heard a prior related matter and formed views
- The decision-maker was involved in a similar case as counsel

### Financial or Property Interest

- Direct financial stake in the outcome
- Indirect financial stake through business or family
- Property interest that would be affected

Even small interests can raise reasonable apprehension.

### Personal Relationships

- Family relationships with parties or counsel
- Close friendships
- Business partnerships

### Public Statements

- Statements about the merits before hearing
- Statements about a party's credibility
- Statements about the outcome
- Publications or speeches on the specific issue

### Conduct During Proceedings

- Cutting off submissions
- Refusing to hear evidence
- Signaling predetermined outcomes
- Consistent one-sided rulings without apparent basis
- Comments suggesting the decision was made in advance
- Extreme deviation from usual practice

### Contextual Factors

- Institutional pressures (e.g. bonuses tied to specific outcomes)
- Structural conflicts (investigator and adjudicator combined)
- Statutory schemes creating incentives

## Institutional Bias

Bias arising from the structure of the decision-making body, not from
any specific individual.

### The Test — 2747-3174 Québec Inc

*2747-3174 Québec Inc v Régie des permis d'alcool* [1996] 3 SCR 919:

Would an informed observer, having examined the institutional framework,
have a reasonable apprehension of bias in a substantial number of cases?

### Common Institutional Bias Concerns

- Investigator and adjudicator functions combined without proper
  separation
- Financial dependency on parties
- Political control over adjudicative appointments
- Structural incentives to favor one party class (e.g. licensing
  bodies dependent on fees from those they regulate)

### Modern Cases

*Ocean Port Hotel v British Columbia (General Manager, Liquor Control
and Licensing Branch)* 2001 SCC 52 — legislature may authorize
structures that would otherwise be problematic; deference to
legislative choice.

*Bell Canada v Canadian Telephone Employees Association* 2003 SCC 36 —
similar deference to legislative structural choices.

## Raising Bias — Procedure

### At the Tribunal

If bias concerns arise during proceedings:

1. Raise the objection at the time
2. State the specific ground
3. Ask the decision-maker to recuse
4. If refused, request the reasons be recorded

Waiver is a real risk — silence in the face of known bias may waive the
objection.

### On Judicial Review

If the bias claim was preserved:

1. Plead bias as a ground of review
2. State the standard (procedural fairness — Baker analysis)
3. Set out the specific factual basis
4. Apply the reasonable apprehension test
5. Address materiality (usually, if bias is established, the decision
   is invalid regardless of merit)

---

## Discipline in Raising Bias

Bias allegations are serious. Making them without foundation:

- Weakens overall credibility
- Attracts adverse costs
- Poisons the tribunal record
- May trigger professional discipline concerns for counsel

Before alleging bias, ask:

- Do I have specific facts, not just adverse rulings?
- Would an informed observer share my concern?
- Is my concern based on actual conduct, not disagreement with the
  outcome?
- Have I ruled out reasonable innocent explanations?

If yes to all, the allegation may be warranted. If any is no, the
argument is likely a losing one.

---

## Common Failures — Bias Claims

### Failure 1 — Adverse Rulings as Evidence

"The arbitrator ruled against me on every objection" is not evidence of
bias. It's evidence the arbitrator disagreed with your objections.

### Failure 2 — Emotional Presentation

"The arbitrator was rude" or "seemed impatient" without more is
insufficient. The test is objective — informed observer, not the
aggrieved party.

### Failure 3 — Speculation

"The arbitrator must have had ex parte communications" without evidence
is speculation. The threshold requires actual evidence.

### Failure 4 — Post-Hoc Reconstruction

Claims of bias not raised at the time weaken. If bias was known, it
should have been raised.

---

## Successful Bias Claims — Elements

Successful bias claims tend to feature:

- Specific, documented conduct or facts
- Contemporaneous objection
- Objective assessment against the reasonable-observer test
- Evidence beyond the aggrieved party's perception
- Cogent connection between the facts and the apprehension

---

## Institutional vs Individual — Strategic Choice

Where both types are present, consider:

- Institutional bias arguments require documentary evidence of the
  structure and its incentives — often easier to establish
- Individual bias arguments require conduct evidence — sometimes
  harder to prove
- Institutional bias arguments implicate the tribunal generally
- Individual bias arguments implicate one adjudicator

For self-represented petitioners, institutional bias arguments may be
harder to sustain without extensive documentary evidence. Individual
bias arguments with specific conduct may be more attainable.

---

## Remedies for Established Bias

- The decision is voidable at the option of the affected party
- The decision is typically set aside and remitted
- Remit is usually to a differently-constituted panel
- In extreme cases (systemic bias), broader remedies may be considered

---

## Bias on Cross-Border Grounds

- Interpreter services deficiencies may implicate procedural fairness
  more than bias per se
- Cultural competence issues may implicate fairness
- Structural inequities in access to representation may implicate
  fairness

These may be raised alongside bias where they contribute to the
apprehension.

---

## Bias in RTB Proceedings — Practical Notes

The RTB is a high-volume tribunal. Practical realities:

- Arbitrators handle many hearings; efficient conduct is expected
- Some conduct that seems rushed may be routine practice, not bias
- Failure to address every argument in reasons may reflect brevity, not
  bias
- Consistent adverse rulings for a party class over many cases may
  raise structural concerns

Before alleging bias in an RTB proceeding, calibrate carefully:

- Is this conduct genuinely outside RTB norms?
- Is the concern about outcome or about process?
- Are the facts documented in the record?

Where the concern is about outcome without process defects, the
argument is likely against the decision on the merits, not on bias.
