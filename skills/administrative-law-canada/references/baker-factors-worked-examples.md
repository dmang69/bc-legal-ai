# Baker Factors — Worked Examples

The five Baker factors applied to specific administrative contexts.

---

## The Five Factors (Recap)

1. Nature of the decision
2. Nature of the statutory scheme
3. Importance of the decision to the individual
4. Legitimate expectations
5. Choices of procedure made by the agency

---

## Example 1 — RTB Eviction Hearing

Standard RTB dispute resolution proceeding on a landlord's notice to
end tenancy.

### Factor 1 — Nature of the Decision

The RTB decision is adjudicative — it resolves a dispute between two
parties based on evidence. Resembles a judicial process.

Weight: strong for higher fairness content.

### Factor 2 — Statutory Scheme

The RTA and Rules of Procedure provide specific procedural rights:
- Notice of hearing
- Right to attend and be heard
- Right to submit evidence
- Right to cross-examine (in participatory hearings)
- Review or judicial review available

Weight: framework provides substantial procedural protections; the
statute sets a floor.

### Factor 3 — Importance to the Individual

An eviction results in loss of home. For most tenants, this is a
severe consequence — dislocation, cost, potential inability to find
alternative housing.

Weight: very strong for higher fairness content.

### Factor 4 — Legitimate Expectations

The RTB has established practices:
- Participatory hearings for contested matters
- Written decisions with reasons
- Time to prepare

Departure from these practices without notice may attract concern.

Weight: reinforces expectations of established procedure.

### Factor 5 — Agency's Choices

RTB has considerable procedural discretion. Its choices are respected
where they are procedurally fair. However, the choices cannot override
factors 1-4.

Weight: does not permit lower fairness where other factors demand
higher.

### Content of Fairness in This Context

- Notice of hearing and issues (satisfied by proper service of notice
  and pleadings)
- Adequate time to prepare (Rules provide this)
- Opportunity to make full submissions
- Opportunity to challenge landlord's evidence
- Consideration of the tenant's evidence and submissions
- Reasoned decision addressing the arguments made
- Availability of review

### Common Fairness Failures at the RTB

- Insufficient time for oral hearing (rushed proceedings)
- Cutting off submissions
- Decision-maker signaling a predetermined outcome
- Failure to address key evidence
- Bald reasons that don't engage with tenant's submissions

## Example 2 — RTB Direct Request

Certain RTB matters proceed by direct request (paper-based) rather than
hearing. Different fairness content.

### Factor 1 — Nature

Less adjudicative, more administrative when uncontested.

### Factor 2 — Statutory Scheme

Specific rules govern direct request. Contested matters typically move
to participatory hearing.

### Factor 3 — Importance

If the decision has serious consequences (eviction), high importance
still applies — but the procedure is designed for uncontested matters.

### Factor 4 — Legitimate Expectations

Direct request procedures are known and consistently applied.

### Factor 5 — Agency's Choices

The choice of direct request is legislatively authorized.

### Content of Fairness

- Notice of the direct request procedure
- Opportunity to object and trigger a hearing if the matter is
  contested
- Clear timeline for objection
- Fair application of the direct request criteria

Where the tenant did not receive adequate notice of the direct request
option, or was denied opportunity to convert to a participatory hearing,
fairness may be breached.

## Example 3 — Professional Discipline Hearing

Regulator hearing on alleged professional misconduct.

### Factor 1 — Nature

Highly adjudicative, quasi-criminal in tone.

### Factor 2 — Statutory Scheme

Professional regulation statutes typically provide extensive
procedural rights.

### Factor 3 — Importance

Loss of professional licence has severe consequences. Highest
importance.

### Factor 4 — Legitimate Expectations

Established procedural practices, precedential decisions.

### Factor 5 — Agency's Choices

Respect for discipline body's choices, but consequences demand
robust procedures.

### Content of Fairness

- Full disclosure of the case to be met
- Right to counsel
- Right to call and cross-examine witnesses
- Right to make submissions
- Right to reasons
- Right to appeal or judicial review

Approaches close to trial-quality fairness.

## Example 4 — Immigration Refugee Determination

Refugee protection claim before the Immigration and Refugee Board.

### Factor 1 — Nature

Adjudicative.

### Factor 2 — Statutory Scheme

Extensive procedural rules under IRPA and IRB regulations.

### Factor 3 — Importance

Highest possible — potentially involves risk to life if refused and
removed.

### Factor 4 — Legitimate Expectations

Established RPD procedures.

### Factor 5 — Agency's Choices

Substantial deference to expert tribunal on procedure.

### Content

Robust fairness — full hearing, right to counsel, interpreter services,
extensive disclosure, reasoned decision.

---

## Example 5 — Zoning or Planning Decision

Municipal planning decision affecting a property.

### Factor 1 — Nature

May be adjudicative (rezoning application) or legislative (bylaw).

### Factor 2 — Statutory Scheme

Local Government Act and municipal bylaws provide the framework.

### Factor 3 — Importance

Significant to the affected property owner but generally not affecting
housing or livelihood as severely as eviction or discipline.

### Factor 4 — Legitimate Expectations

Established public hearing processes.

### Factor 5 — Agency's Choices

Municipalities have considerable procedural discretion.

### Content

Public notice, opportunity to make submissions, transparent
consideration. Fairness content is more modest than adjudicative
proceedings but still meaningful.

## The Pattern

Baker analysis shows:

- Adjudicative + high stakes → high fairness content
- Administrative + low stakes → lower fairness content
- Established practice → sets expectations
- Statutory rights → floor, not ceiling

For any tribunal decision, run the five factors. The resulting content
tells you what procedures were required. Deviation from those procedures
supports a fairness argument.

---

## Application in a JR

For a procedural fairness ground in a JR petition:

```
1. Identify the specific procedure the tribunal followed (or didn't)

2. Apply the Baker factors to determine what fairness required in
   this specific context

3. Compare the required procedure to what actually happened

4. Show the gap

5. Argue that the gap breaches the duty of fairness

6. Show materiality — that the breach affected the outcome (usually
   presumed for significant procedural defects)
```

The Baker analysis is not a formulaic checklist. It's a structured way
of asking: given everything about this decision and this tribunal, what
fairness was required, and did the tribunal deliver it?
