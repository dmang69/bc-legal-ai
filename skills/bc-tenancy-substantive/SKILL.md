## Module 2: Bad-Faith Move Detector ⭐

### Renovations or repairs requiring vacancy — director's order under s. 49.2

**Potential bad-faith pattern:** A landlord asserts that major work requires vacancy, but the proposed work is cosmetic, unsupported by permits, or followed by re-rental at a higher rent.

**Official map (BC Laws consolidation current to July 14, 2026):** For renovations or repairs requiring vacancy, **s. 49.2** provides an application-for-order route and specifies statutory conditions. **Section 51.2** addresses a right of first refusal in qualifying circumstances; **s. 51.3** addresses compensation where no right of first refusal is given. Do not collapse these distinct provisions or assume that every renovation engages a same-rent return right. Verify the current Act, regulations, order, tenancy/building facts, eligibility, notice requirements, and remedy before making a claim.

**Detection signals:**

- No building permits applied for or issued  
- Work is cosmetic — does not require vacant possession  
- Unit re-listed at higher rent after eviction  
- Pattern of renovation notices without completed work  

**Response:** Dispute the notice + demand all permits and renovation plans in writing.

→ `references/renoviction-defense.md`

### Demoviction (Four Month Notice — demolition)

Landlord must have an **actual demolition permit** and approved development. Speculative future demolition is not enough.

### Caretaker / employment housing — s. 48

**Potential issue:** The parties dispute whether a rental unit was provided for the term of genuine caretaker, manager, superintendent, or other employment.

**Official map:** RTA **s. 48** governs specified end-of-employment notices and contains factual and notice requirements. The analysis must begin with the statutory definition of tenancy/tenancy agreement, exclusions in s. 4, the agreement, payment and employment records, why the unit was provided, whether employment ended, good faith where required, and the current notice requirements. Labels alone are not conclusive, but payment alone does not decide the issue either. **Section 5**, not s. 6, provides that the Act cannot be avoided.

### Common disputed-practice issue map

| Issue | Correct starting point | Required verification |
|--------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| City/bylaw charge allocated to tenant | Agreement, enabling bylaw/statute, RTA s. 5, rent/services provisions, damages principles | Who is legally liable; whether contractual allocation is authorized and enforceable |
| Administrative/application/processing fee | RTA s. 15 for prohibited application-type fees; agreement and regulations for other fees | Do not use s. 8, which appoints the director |
| Strata fine or charge | SPA/bylaws, notice and hearing process, tenancy agreement, RTA s. 5 | Do not assume every pass-through is automatically void |
| Deposit retained | RTA ss. 23–24, 35–39 and regulation | Inspections, forwarding address, consent, application/order, timing |
| Entry without required notice | RTA s. 29; quiet enjoyment s. 28 | Statutory exception, notice contents/timing, consent, emergency |
| Rent increase | RTA ss. 40–43.1 and regulation | Form, timing, amount, service, effective date, available remedy |
| Adverse action after complaint | Facts, applicable notice ground, RTA ss. 5–7/28/47–49.2, human-rights or other law as engaged | There is no general RTA s. 47.1 retaliation provision in the current consolidation |
| “Lease says you must pay X” | RTA s. 5 plus the specific substantive provision and regulation | Contract terms are not void merely because they impose a payment; identify the actual prohibition |

## Module 3: Eviction Defence ⭐ TIME-CRITICAL

**Miss a deadline and the path narrows hard.**

### Notice types & dispute windows (working — verify forms)

| Notice | Grounds | Dispute window (typical) |
|--------|---------|---------------------------|
| **Ten Day** (often RTB-30) | Non-payment of rent | **5 days** to pay **or** dispute |
| **One Month / Cause** (often RTB-33) | Repeated / serious cause | **10 days** to dispute |
| **Two Month** (often RTB-32) | Landlord’s use of property | **15 days** to dispute |
| **Four Month** (often RTB-32) | Demolition / renovation / conversion | **15 days** to dispute |

Deadline runs from **date of service**. Add **deemed-service** days for mailed notices (**s. 89** — verify).

### Procedural defects (often decisive)

- Wrong form for stated reason  
- Incorrect notice period for tenancy type  
- Address on notice ≠ actual unit designation  
- Not properly served under **ss. 88–89**  
- Reason legally insufficient on its face  
- Landlord accepted rent after alleged violation (**waiver** theory — fact-specific)  

### Substantive defences

| Defence | Core idea |
|---------|-----------|
| **Improper purpose / bad faith / legally insufficient ground** | Test the actual notice provision and evidence; do not cite current s. 47.1 as a general retaliation section |
| **Bad faith renoviction/demoviction** | Module 2 |
| **Caretaker scam** | Actually a tenant |
| **Non-payment disputed** | Paid / refused / amount wrong |
| **Cause weak** | Minor, remediated, or fabricated |
| **Discrimination** | Protected ground → `bc-tenancy-advanced` (BCHRT) |
| ***Res judicata*** | Same landlord, unit, grounds — already lost |

### On receiving a notice — do this now

1. Identify notice type + verify correct form  
2. **Calendar dispute deadline immediately**  
3. Check address designation vs actual unit  
4. Preserve all communications (screenshots, emails, voicemails)  
5. Gather payment records  
6. File tenant application (**often RTB-12** — verify) before deadline  
7. If **Order of Possession** issued → stay strategy (`bc-judicial-review-guide`) — **`[INDEPENDENT COUNSEL RECOMMENDED]`**

## Module 4: Repairs & Maintenance

**ss. 32–33:** Good repair + health/safety. Emergency → immediate. Routine: 7–14 days often treated as reasonable (not a hard code limit — verify practice). Service reductions (heat, hot water) = breach.

**Mold:** Health hazard; RTB priority. Cite **s. 32(1)(b)** if that is the current health/safety limb — **`[VERIFY]`**. Dated photos + medical impact if any.

### Tenant remedies (stack these)

1. Written repair request — always first (paper trail)  
2. RTB repair order  
3. Rent reduction (retroactive from defect date)  
4. Monetary compensation (damage, health costs)  
5. Termination for uninhabitable conditions (**s. 36**) — high bar  

### Evidence to build

- Dated photos/videos of every defect  
- Written requests with delivery confirmation  
- Landlord responses or documented silence  
- Inspector/bylaw reports, medical records, repair receipts

## Module 5: Disputed Fees, Charges, Fines, and Damages ⭐

**Critical correction:** Current RTA **s. 8 appoints the director**. It does not create a “rent plus deposits only” rule and must not be cited to invalidate a charge.

### Issue-by-issue analysis

1. Identify what the amount legally is: rent, deposit, prescribed fee, utility obligation, service/facility charge, liquidated damages, compensation claim, strata amount, or government fine.
2. Read the tenancy agreement and standard terms, then test the term under **s. 5** and the specific RTA/regulation provision engaged.
3. For application/processing-type fees, examine **s. 15**. For deposits, use ss. 17–21 and 23–24/35–39 as applicable. For rent/services, use the relevant Part 2/3 provisions.
4. For strata or government amounts, verify the enabling enactment/bylaw, who is primarily liable, procedural prerequisites, and whether contractual indemnity/allocation is lawful.
5. Distinguish a landlord's damages claim from a unilateral deposit deduction. A party may assert damages without already holding an RTB order, but collection, deduction, proof, mitigation, and forum rules differ.
6. Identify the actual statutory remedy and burden. Do not promise a declaration, repayment, penalty, or retaliation remedy without verified authority.

A charge is not automatically valid because it appears in a lease, and it is not automatically void merely because it is not labelled rent or deposit.

## Module 6: Rent Increases

### Valid increase (working requirements)

- **3 full calendar months’** written notice (often **RTB-7** form — verify)  
- Amount ≤ current **Rent Increase Guideline**  
- **Once per 12-month** period only  

### Challenge grounds

Insufficient notice · exceeds guideline · second increase in 12 months · improper service  

**File RTB dispute within 30 days** of receiving the notice (verify).

### Above-guideline (often s. 44)

Landlord applies to RTB + financial records. Tenant files Response and attends. Demand full disclosure of capital expenditure documents.

→ `references/rent-increase-guideline.md`

## Module 7: Security & Pet Deposits

| Rule | Detail (verify) |
|------|-----------------|
| Maximum | ½ month’s rent each type |
| Return deadline | **15 days** after end of tenancy or receipt of forwarding address |
| Withholding | Written tenant consent **or** RTB monetary order only |
| Wrongful withholding penalty | Up to **double** the deposit — often **s. 38(6)** |

**Condition inspections:** Landlord failure to conduct move-in/out inspection can **lose right to claim damages against deposit** (confirm current rule). Document who/when/what; keep signed copy.

## Workflow (substantive matter)

1. Calendar any **notice dispute deadline** first.  
2. Identify notice type / charge / defect under Modules 1–3.  
3. Run **bad-faith detector** (Module 2).  
4. Build proof list → hand to `bc-tenancy-procedure` for packaging.  
5. Draft remedies: void notice · repayment · repair · compensation · first refusal.  
6. If decision already issued: review / JR path via advanced + JR guide.
