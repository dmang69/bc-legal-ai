# Adverse Action After a Tenant Exercises Rights

> Legal information only. Verify the current RTA and regulations on BC Laws and identify the actual statutory notice ground or remedy. Current RTA s. 47.1 is **not** a general retaliation prohibition.

## Core issue

Close timing between a tenant's repair request, complaint, organizing, or RTB filing and later landlord action may support an inference about purpose or credibility. Timing alone does not create a standalone RTA cause of action or automatically invalidate a notice.

## Required analysis

1. Identify the challenged act: notice to end tenancy, entry, service reduction, rent increase, harassment, claim, or other conduct.
2. Identify the exact current provision governing that act.
3. Test every statutory element, notice requirement, good-faith requirement, burden, and remedy.
4. Build a chronology and compare stated reasons with contemporaneous records.
5. Consider alternative explanations and evidence that supports or weakens each one.
6. Assess whether quiet enjoyment, discrimination/human-rights law, procedural fairness, abuse of process, or another doctrine is independently engaged.
7. Do not cite s. 47.1 unless the current text genuinely applies to the issue being analyzed.

## Proof pattern

```text
[Date] Tenant exercised a right — source/exhibit
[Date] Landlord learned of it — source/exhibit
[Date] Challenged action occurred — source/exhibit
[Date] Stated reason and supporting records — source/exhibit
[Date] Contrary or comparator evidence — source/exhibit
```

## Output labels

`[FACT]` dated event with exhibit · `[ALLEGATION]` retaliatory purpose · `[INFERENCE]` timing/credibility inference · `[LAW]` actual verified provision · `[ARGUMENT]` statutory ground not proved or improper purpose · `[REMEDY]` remedy authorized by that provision
