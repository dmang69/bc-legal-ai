# Argument Templates — Skeleton Structures

Reusable structures for common argument types.

---

## Structure 1 — Judicial Review Ground

```
GROUND [N]: [name — e.g. "Patent Unreasonableness on Attribution"]

Standard of Review
  Under ATA s.58, the standard for [type of finding] is [patent unreasonableness /
  procedural fairness / jurisdictional error]. This means [test].

The Impugned Finding
  The arbitrator found that [precise quotation with paragraph reference].

Why This Is [Patently Unreasonable / A Breach of PF / Jurisdictional Error]
  1. [Element of the test] — [how it's met]
  2. [Element of the test] — [how it's met]
  3. [Element of the test] — [how it's met]

The Anticipated Response
  The Attorney General will argue [X]. This response fails because [Y].

Remedy Sought on This Ground
  [Set aside / remit / declare / quash]
```

## Structure 2 — Thesis Chapter Argument

```
CHAPTER [N]: [title]

The Claim
  This chapter argues that [precise claim].

Why This Claim Matters
  [What's at stake if the claim is true; what it enables or resolves]

The Argument Structure
  This chapter proceeds in [N] steps:
  1. Establish [premise]
  2. Establish [premise]
  3. Show [warrant]
  4. Derive [claim]

Section 1 — [First premise]
  [Toulmin structure: claim, grounds, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal]

Section 2 — [Second premise]
  [Same]

Section 3 — [Warrant]
  [Explaining why premises 1 and 2 support the claim]

Section 4 — [Conclusion]
  [Restated claim + implications]

Anticipated Objections
  Objection 1: [strongest counter]
  Response: [why the counter fails or is limited]

Objection 2: [second counter]
  Response: [same]

Chapter Conclusion
  [Restatement + handoff to next chapter]
```

## Structure 3 — Technical Proposal

```
PROPOSAL: [what you're proposing]

Problem Statement
  [What problem exists, why it matters, why current solutions are inadequate]

Proposed Solution
  [Precise description of what you're proposing]

Why This Solution
  1. [Property A of the solution] → [property A addresses X part of problem]
  2. [Property B] → [addresses Y]
  3. [Property C] → [addresses Z]

Alternative Approaches Considered
  Alternative 1: [what and why rejected]
  Alternative 2: [same]

Risks and Mitigations
  Risk: [what could go wrong]
    Mitigation: [how you'd address it]
  Risk: [second]
    Mitigation: [same]

Success Criteria
  [How you'd know if it worked]

Implementation Path
  [Concrete next steps]
```

## Structure 4 — Response to Opposing Submission

```
RESPONSE TO [document title]

Points of Agreement
  [What we do not dispute — narrow this as much as honesty allows]

Points of Dispute
  Point 1 — [opponent's claim]
    Our position: [response]
    Reasoning: [why]
    Authority: [supporting]

  Point 2 — [same]

Legal or Factual Errors in Opposing Submission
  Error 1: [what they got wrong] — [correction with authority]
  Error 2: [same]

Reframing Where Needed
  [If opponent's framing itself is misleading, restate the actual question]

Our Position, Restated
  [Clean summary of what we're asking for and why]
```

## Structure 5 — Steel-Man First Argument

Use when you know the reader is predisposed against your position.

```
The Case Against My Position
  The strongest form of the objection to what I'm about to argue is [X].
  This objection is not a straw man. It rests on [reasoning] and would be
  decisive if [conditions].

Where That Case Fails
  However, [conditions] do not hold in this context because [reasons].
  Specifically:
  1. [Reason]
  2. [Reason]
  3. [Reason]

My Position
  Once the objection is properly bounded, the correct position is [claim].

The Positive Case
  [Toulmin or IRAC structure supporting the claim]
```

The steel-man first move disarms the reader who was preparing objections
and shows you've done the work. Powerful in academic and appellate contexts.
