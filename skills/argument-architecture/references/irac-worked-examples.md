# IRAC++ Worked Examples

Full IRAC++ structure applied to real fact patterns.

---

## Example 1 — Bad-Faith s.51 Notice

**I — Issue**
Whether a landlord's Two Month Notice under s.51 is invalid where the
landlord's post-eviction conduct shows no intention to occupy the unit
for the stated purpose.

**R — Rule**
Under RTA s.51(1), a landlord may end a tenancy for landlord's use only
where the landlord intends in good faith to occupy the unit.

**R+ — Sub-rule**
"Good faith" under RTB Policy Guideline 2A requires an honest intention
at the time the notice is served, without ulterior motive.

**R++ — Authority**
- RTA s.51(1)
- RTA s.51.4 (compensation on failure to occupy)
- Gichuru v. Palmar Properties Ltd. 2011 BCSC (bad faith test)
- RTB Policy Guideline 2A

**A — Application**
The landlord's affidavit stated intention to occupy. The evidence shows:
(a) the landlord did not move in within a reasonable time, (b) the unit
was re-rented at a materially higher rent within [X] months, (c) the
landlord's own utility records show no occupancy consumption during the
stated period. Each element of Gichuru's bad-faith test is met.

**A+ — Counter**
The landlord will argue that circumstances changed after the notice was
served, so the initial intention was genuine. This fails because the
timeline shows [specific evidence of pre-notice intention to re-rent].

**C — Conclusion**
The s.51 notice was served in bad faith. It is invalid. The tenancy is
reinstated and compensation under s.51.4 is owed.

## Example 2 — Judicial Review of Patent Unreasonableness

**I — Issue**
Whether the arbitrator's finding on [specific factual matter] is patently
unreasonable within the meaning of ATA s.58.

**R — Rule**
Under ATA s.58, this court may set aside a finding of fact only where it
is patently unreasonable — meaning no evidence supports it, or the finding
is one that a reasonable arbitrator could not have made on the record.

**R+ — Sub-rule**
The standard, as clarified in [authority], is not correctness or even
reasonableness in the ordinary sense. It requires a finding so defective
that no reasonable person acquainted with the evidence could have made it.

**R++ — Authority**
- ATA s.58(2)(a) and s.58(3)
- Speckling v. British Columbia (WCB) 2005 BCCA 80
- Vavilov 2019 SCC 65 (contrasting standards discussion)

**A — Application**
The arbitrator found [X]. The record contains [evidence contradicting X].
The arbitrator did not explain how [contradicting evidence] was weighed.
No reasonable arbitrator on this record could have reached [X] without
addressing [contradicting evidence].

**A+ — Counter**
The Attorney General will argue the arbitrator's finding is entitled to
deference and that this court cannot reweigh evidence. That is correct as
a general proposition, but does not extend to findings that fail the
patent unreasonableness threshold — which this one does because [specific
reason distinguishing this from ordinary reweighing].

**C — Conclusion**
The finding is patently unreasonable. It should be set aside and the
matter remitted.

## The Failure Modes to Avoid

### Failure Mode: Conclusion in the Issue
Bad: "Whether the arbitrator acted unreasonably in ignoring evidence."
Better: "Whether the arbitrator's finding is patently unreasonable within
the meaning of ATA s.58 where [neutral description of the record]."

The bad version already assumes what you're trying to prove.

### Failure Mode: Paraphrased Rule
Bad: "The arbitrator has to consider the evidence."
Better: "Under ATA s.58(3), a finding is patently unreasonable where..."

The bad version signals to the court that you don't know the statute.
The better version signals that you do.

### Failure Mode: Facts Without Elements
Bad: "The landlord didn't move in and rented to someone else."
Better: "The failure to occupy within a reasonable time (element 1 of
Gichuru), combined with re-rental at higher rent (element 3), establishes
each required component of bad faith."

The bad version leaves it to the court to connect the dots. The better
version does it for them.
