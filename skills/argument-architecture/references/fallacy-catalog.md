# Fallacy Catalog — Expanded

Module 8 fallacies, expanded with legal/academic contexts.

---

## Ad Hominem

**Form:** Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument.
**Legal example:** "Opposing counsel is inexperienced with this area."
**Antidote:** Argue the position, not the person. If credibility IS the
issue (e.g. impeachment of a witness), frame it as evidence of unreliability,
not as personal attack.

## Straw Man

**Form:** Misrepresenting the opposing position to make it easier to attack.
**Legal example:** "The petitioner asks this court to overturn every RTB
decision it disagrees with." — when the petitioner actually asks for
review of a specific patent unreasonableness.
**Antidote:** Steel-man before rebutting. State the opposing position in
its strongest form. If your rebuttal still holds, it's a real rebuttal.

## Slippery Slope

**Form:** Asserting a chain of consequences without warrant for each link.
**Legal example:** "If we allow this exception, no lease will be enforceable."
**Antidote:** Legitimate consequence arguments name each step and warrant it.
Slippery slope is when the steps are asserted without justification.

## Appeal to Authority (Illegitimate)

**Form:** Citing authority in a domain where the authority isn't qualified,
or citing authority where the reasoning behind the authority is what matters.
**Legal example:** Citing a leading textbook without noting that the section
has been overtaken by more recent case law.
**Antidote:** Cite authority for what it authoritatively covers. Note where
authority is relevant and where reasoning must take over.

## Appeal to Popularity

**Form:** Because many people believe X, X is true.
**Academic example:** "It's widely accepted that..." without citing why.
**Antidote:** Popularity is evidence in some domains (linguistic usage,
market behavior) and irrelevant in others (physical facts). Know which
you're in.

## Circular Reasoning

**Form:** Using the conclusion as a premise.
**Legal example:** "The notice was in bad faith because the landlord acted
in bad faith." — the conclusion (bad faith) is used to prove itself.
**Antidote:** Every premise should be independently establishable. Test each
premise: can I prove it without invoking the conclusion?

## False Dilemma

**Form:** Presenting two options when more exist.
**Legal example:** "Either the arbitrator was correct or the process was
corrupt." — ignores the possibility of good-faith error.
**Antidote:** Enumerate the actual option space. If you're presenting a
binary, verify it's actually binary.

## Hasty Generalization

**Form:** Sweeping claim from limited examples.
**Legal example:** "This arbitrator always sides with landlords." — based
on three cases.
**Antidote:** Match the strength of the claim to the size and representativeness
of the evidence base.

## Post Hoc

**Form:** Because B followed A, A caused B.
**Legal example:** "After the notice was served, the tenant became ill,
so the notice caused the illness."
**Antidote:** Distinguish sequence from causation. Causation requires more
than temporal ordering — it requires a mechanism, exclusion of confounders,
or repeated demonstration.

## Equivocation

**Form:** Using one word in two different senses in the same argument.
**Legal example:** "The landlord had a 'right' to occupy [legal right],
therefore the landlord was 'right' [morally justified] to serve the notice."
**Antidote:** Define terms early. Where a word has multiple senses, pick
one and stick to it. If two senses are both relevant, name them
distinctly.

## Red Herring

**Form:** Introducing an irrelevant point to distract.
**Legal example:** In response to a bad-faith allegation, discussing the
landlord's charitable donations.
**Antidote:** Every point should serve the argument. Points that don't
serve are cuts.

## Tu Quoque

**Form:** Deflecting a criticism by pointing at the critic.
**Legal example:** "The tenant is also in breach of X, so their complaint
about my breach of Y should fail."
**Antidote:** Sometimes the parties' respective conduct is genuinely
relevant (e.g. clean hands, mutual breach). But it's not a defense to the
merits of the underlying claim — those must be addressed on their own terms.

## The Legitimate Cousins

Each fallacy has a legitimate cousin that looks similar but isn't fallacious.

| Fallacy | Legitimate Cousin |
|---|---|
| Ad hominem | Impeachment of witness credibility with evidence |
| Appeal to authority | Citing binding precedent |
| Slippery slope | Consequence argument with warranted steps |
| Appeal to popularity | Market or linguistic evidence |
| Tu quoque | Clean hands doctrine (in equity) |
| Hasty generalization | Sufficient-sample statistical argument |

The fallacy is the misapplication, not the form. Know both.
