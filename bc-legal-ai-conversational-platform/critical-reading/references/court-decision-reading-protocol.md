# Court Decision Reading Protocol

Full protocol for reading judicial decisions.

---

## Read 1 — Orientation (10-15 minutes)

Goal: know the shape.

```
Court:              [level, jurisdiction]
Date:               [year]
Justices:           [names, majority / dissent split if any]
Parties:            [who vs who]
Type:               [trial / appeal / judicial review / motion]
Nature:             [what kind of case]
```

Skim to identify:
- The issue(s) the court identified
- The result (allowed / dismissed / partial)
- Any dissents

Do NOT try to understand the reasoning yet. Just get the shape.

---

## Read 2 — Structural Decomposition (30-60 minutes)

Section by section, identify what work each section is doing.

### Standard Sections

Most decisions include some subset of:

```
Introduction / Overview
  Purpose: frame the case
  Load-bearing?: usually not, but sets tone

Facts
  Purpose: establish the factual record
  Load-bearing?: yes — the "findings of fact" section
  Read for: which facts are asserted vs found, which are disputed

Procedural History
  Purpose: what happened at prior stages
  Load-bearing?: usually not, but check for procedural issues

Legal Framework
  Purpose: state the applicable law
  Load-bearing?: yes — the standards and tests
  Read for: how the court states the tests, what authorities are relied on

Analysis / Reasoning
  Purpose: apply law to facts
  Load-bearing?: highest — this is where the case is won or lost
  Read for: the ratio, the specific findings, the logical chain

Disposition
  Purpose: the order
  Load-bearing?: yes — this is what actually binds
  Read for: exactly what was ordered
```

---

## Read 3 — Ratio Extraction (30-60 minutes)

The ratio is the load-bearing reasoning. Everything else is obiter.

### Ratio Extraction Protocol

```
1. Identify the legal question
2. Identify the test the court used
3. Identify the specific finding on each element of the test
4. Identify the conclusion drawn from those findings
5. Identify anything necessary to that reasoning

Everything else is obiter.
```

### Ratio vs Obiter — Common Confusions

- Long factual recitations are usually NOT ratio; they're context
- Rejected arguments the court discusses are usually obiter
- Comments about "in another case, we might have..." are always obiter
- Comments about policy implications are usually obiter
- The application of law to the specific facts of THIS case IS ratio
- Explicit statements of a legal test ARE ratio

### Common Ratio Extraction Failures

- Treating a court's approving citation as endorsement — it's not
  necessarily ratio unless the court applied the cited proposition
- Treating a court's aside as binding — asides aren't
- Missing the ratio because it's stated once, quickly, in the middle of
  a long paragraph
- Treating the disposition as the ratio — the disposition is what was
  ordered, the ratio is why

## Standard of Review — Always Extract

For any decision you're reviewing (as a JR petitioner, appellant, or
counsel citing it), the standard of review is critical.

```
Standard applied:           [correctness / reasonableness / patent unreasonableness]
Source of standard:         [statute / common law / precedent]
How stated:                 [exact language the court uses]
How applied:                [where in the reasoning the standard actually
                             shapes the analysis]
```

Standards of review are the load-bearing meta-rule of the decision. A
decision reviewing under reasonableness that reads like correctness is
often a signal of error.

---

## Findings of Fact — Extract Separately

Facts found by the court are treated differently from facts asserted by
parties.

```
FINDING                             │ SUPPORT                    │ ADMITTED?
────────────────────────────────────┼────────────────────────────┼──────────
[what the court found as fact]      │ [evidence relied on]       │ [Y/N]
```

Findings of fact matter because:
- On appeal or JR, they generally receive deference
- They shape what happens on remand if the decision is set aside
- They may support or defeat future arguments in related proceedings

---

## Dissents

If there is a dissent:

```
Dissent by:         [judge]
Points of departure:
  1. [where the dissent disagrees with the majority]
  2. [same]
  3. [same]

Would the dissent's reasoning yield a different disposition?
  [Y/N — explain]

Is the dissent likely to influence future decisions?
  [Y/N — based on subsequent citation patterns if known]
```

Dissents are not binding but are often cited in subsequent argument.
Extract them separately.

## The Post-Read Summary

For the file — one-page summary you'll refer back to:

```
CASE:               [citation]
COURT:              [level]
ISSUE:              [in your own words, one sentence]
HELD:               [in your own words, one sentence]
STANDARD OF REVIEW: [one line]
TEST APPLIED:       [one line, or citation to the test source]
KEY FACTS:          [3-5 bullets]
RATIO:              [2-3 sentences maximum]
NOTABLE OBITER:     [if any]
DISSENT:            [if any]
RELEVANCE TO YOUR WORK:
  - [how you'd use this case]
  - [what proposition it supports]
  - [what to watch out for if citing it]
```

Store this with the case in your BOA workflow (see canlii-boa-builder).
