# Contract Reading Protocol

Contracts don't reward speed. They reward slow, structured reading.

---

## The Four Reads

Contracts genuinely warrant four reads for anything important.

### Read 1 — Business Terms

Extract WHAT is being agreed:
- Parties
- Subject matter
- Consideration (what's being exchanged)
- Term (how long)
- Territory (where applicable)
- Key obligations of each party
- Key rights of each party

This is what a business person cares about. If you can't state these in
plain English after Read 1, you don't understand the deal.

### Read 2 — Legal Structure

Extract HOW the agreement operates as a legal instrument:
- Governing law
- Jurisdiction / forum
- Dispute resolution mechanism
- Notice provisions
- Assignment / change of control
- Termination triggers and consequences
- Survival clauses (what continues after termination)
- Amendment provisions
- Entire agreement / integration clauses

### Read 3 — Risk Allocation

Extract WHO BEARS WHAT RISK:
- Representations and warranties (asserted current facts)
- Covenants (promised future conduct)
- Indemnities (who pays if what goes wrong)
- Limitations of liability (caps)
- Exclusions (what's NOT covered)
- Insurance requirements
- Force majeure

Risk allocation is where the negotiation happens. Read 3 is the read that
matters most for legal review.

### Read 4 — The Cross-Reference and Definitions Check

Every "defined term" (usually capitalized) has a specific meaning. Every
"as defined in Section X" is a cross-reference. Verify:

- Every defined term is actually defined
- Every defined term is USED consistently with its definition
- Every cross-reference points to a real section
- No terms have shifted meaning between sections

Read 4 catches drafting errors. In a well-drafted contract, this read is
uneventful. In a hastily drafted one, this read finds most of the
problems.

## The Section-Purpose Table for Contracts

```
SECTION | PURPOSE                          | RISK LEVEL
────────┼──────────────────────────────────┼───────────
1       | Definitions                      | Low (mechanical)
2       | Grant of rights                  | High (the core deal)
3       | Consideration                    | Medium
4       | Term and termination             | High
5       | Reps and warranties              | High
6       | Covenants                        | Medium
7       | Indemnification                  | High
8       | Limitation of liability          | High
9       | Confidentiality                  | Medium
10      | Governing law and forum          | Medium
11      | Miscellaneous                    | Low (usually)
```

The high-risk sections warrant slow reading. The low-risk sections still
need to be read (Section 11 sometimes contains surprises), but the pace
can be faster.

## The Attack-Surface Scan

For any contract you're negotiating (not just signing), scan for:

```
□ ONE-WAY OBLIGATIONS
  Is a promise on your side matched by a corresponding promise on theirs?
  Or is the burden asymmetric?

□ VAGUE STANDARDS
  "Reasonable efforts", "commercially reasonable", "material" — these are
  vague on purpose. Are they vague in your favor or theirs?

□ CARVE-OUTS
  "Except as provided in..." — the exception often eats the rule.

□ DEFINITIONAL SLIPPAGE
  A word defined narrowly in one section, used broadly in another.

□ SURVIVING OBLIGATIONS
  Which obligations continue after termination? Confidentiality typically.
  What else?

□ AUTOMATIC RENEWAL
  Does the contract auto-renew unless you affirmatively terminate?
  What's the notice window?

□ ASSIGNMENT
  Can they assign to a third party without your consent? Can you?

□ INDEMNIFICATION SCOPE
  What triggers your indemnity? Only your breach? Or also third-party
  claims?

□ LIABILITY CAPS
  Is the cap symmetric? Are there carve-outs from the cap? Which side
  benefits from the cap?

□ CHANGE OF CONTROL
  If they're acquired, what changes? If you're acquired, what changes?
```

Each of these is a common attack surface. In a well-drafted contract from
your side, they're all in your favor. In a contract from the other side,
they're all in theirs. Contract negotiation is largely working these
levers.

## The Redline Discipline

If you're making changes:

```
□ Every change addresses a specific concern
□ Every change has a rationale you can articulate
□ Every change is defensible if challenged
□ You've prioritized changes — some are must-have, some are nice-to-have
□ You've considered whether accepting their position is acceptable
```

Redlining every clause weakens your negotiating position — signals you
don't know what matters. Redline strategically. Save the fights for the
clauses that matter most.

---

## The Reading-for-Later Discipline

Contracts get referred to later — sometimes years later. Read with that
in mind.

Notes to keep with the executed contract:

```
□ Key dates (renewal notice deadlines, milestone dates, term expiration)
□ Contact information for notices under the contract
□ Points of ambiguity we accepted (and what we understood them to mean)
□ Provisions that were negotiated (and the compromise reached)
□ Provisions that were NOT negotiated (accepted as-is)
□ Cross-references to other agreements (side letters, amendments)
```

Future-you will need to know these when a question arises. Present-you is
the only person in a position to write them down.
