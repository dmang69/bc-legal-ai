# Academic Paper Reading Protocol

Most academic papers are ~80% context and ~20% contribution. Extract the
contribution efficiently.

---

## The Three-Pass Method

### Pass 1 — The 5-Minute Scan

Read in this order:
1. Title
2. Abstract
3. Introduction (first paragraph, last paragraph)
4. Section headings
5. Figures and captions
6. Conclusion

After the 5-minute scan, you should be able to answer:
- What's the claim?
- What's the domain?
- Is this worth a deeper read?

If no to the last, stop. Not every paper deserves your attention.

### Pass 2 — The 30-Minute Read

Read the whole paper, but strategically:
- Skim the literature review (know what's cited, don't memorize)
- Read the methodology carefully (this is where papers stand or fall)
- Read the results carefully (what did they actually find)
- Read the discussion (what they think it means — often overreach)
- Skim the conclusion (usually restates the abstract)

After the 30-minute read, you should be able to:
- Reconstruct the argument
- Identify the methodology
- Evaluate the evidence
- Spot the main weaknesses

### Pass 3 — The Deep Read (as needed)

If you're going to build on the paper, cite it heavily, or challenge it,
read a third time:
- Reproduce the methodology in your head
- Check the citations for propositions the paper makes
- Read the references section for context
- Note anything you'd want to verify independently

Most papers don't warrant a Pass 3. Reserve it for papers that are
load-bearing for your own work.

---

## The Extraction Structure

After Pass 2, produce:

```
PAPER: [citation]
──────────────────
CLAIM:               [what the paper argues, one sentence]

METHOD:              [how they made the claim, one sentence]

EVIDENCE:            [what evidence supports it, one sentence]

DISTINCTIVE MOVE:    [what's novel here vs prior work]

WEAKNESSES:
  - [structural weakness in methodology]
  - [weakness in evidence]
  - [overreach in interpretation]

STRENGTHS:
  - [what the paper does well]
  - [what makes the claim credible]

USEFULNESS TO ME:
  - [how I'd cite this]
  - [what proposition it supports]
  - [what I'd need to check before relying on it]

DO NOT USE FOR:
  - [propositions the paper doesn't actually support]
  - [claims that are the paper's overreach]
```

## Common Overreach Patterns

Academic papers frequently overreach in the discussion section. Watch
for:

### 1. Correlation → Causation

"X and Y are correlated" is a finding. "X causes Y" is a claim. Watch
for the slippage.

### 2. In Vitro → In Vivo

Findings in a controlled setting extrapolated to a real one without
justification.

### 3. Small N → Big Conclusion

"In our sample of 15..." → "This suggests broadly..."

### 4. Statistical Significance → Practical Significance

p < 0.05 doesn't mean the effect is large enough to matter.

### 5. Model → Reality

"Our model predicts..." is a claim about the model. Whether the model
tracks reality is a separate question.

### 6. Ad-hoc Explanations

Post-hoc rationalizations of unexpected results dressed as findings.

Papers that overreach in the discussion are still often useful — for
their METHOD or their SPECIFIC FINDINGS. But don't cite them for the
overreach.

## The Citation Discipline

When citing an academic paper in your own work:

```
□ Am I citing the paper for a claim the paper actually makes?
□ Am I citing the paper for a claim the paper is entitled to make?
    (i.e. the methodology supports the claim)
□ Have I read the paper, or only the abstract?
□ Do I have the specific page or section where the proposition appears?
□ Are there subsequent papers that update, extend, or refute this one?
```

Two failure modes to avoid:

**Failure Mode 1 — Chain Citation**
Citing a paper because another paper cited it, without having read the
original. If the intermediate paper mischaracterized the original, you
inherit the error.

**Failure Mode 2 — Abstract Citation**
Citing based on the abstract. Abstracts often oversell.

Read what you cite. If you can't, hedge or cite the review that covers
it (openly).

## Adversarial Reading

For papers you disagree with or need to critique:

```
STRUCTURAL CHALLENGES
  □ Is the methodology appropriate for the claim?
  □ Is the sample representative?
  □ Are confounders addressed?
  □ Are the statistical methods appropriate?
  □ Is the analysis reproducible from the reported data?

EVIDENTIARY CHALLENGES
  □ Does the evidence actually support the claim?
  □ Could the evidence support alternative explanations?
  □ Have contradicting studies been addressed?
  □ Is the effect size large enough to matter?

INTERPRETIVE CHALLENGES
  □ Does the discussion overreach?
  □ Are the policy or practical implications warranted?
  □ Are limitations acknowledged in proportion to their weight?

CITATION CHALLENGES
  □ Does the paper accurately characterize its cited sources?
  □ Are counterarguments in the literature addressed?
  □ Is the paper positioning itself relative to the strongest prior work,
    or only convenient prior work?
```

Adversarial reading isn't hostile reading — it's honest reading.
Papers benefit from being read this way, and readers benefit even more.
